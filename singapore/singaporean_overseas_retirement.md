## Executive Summary

Retiring overseas is increasingly attractive for Singaporeans seeking lower cost of living, different lifestyle, or proximity to family abroad. However, the decision involves complex tradeoffs across finances, healthcare, legal standing, and lifestyle. This guide consolidates all major considerations.

**Key takeaways:**
- CPF LIFE payouts can be received while living in any country
- MediShield Life covers overseas emergencies only; you'll need international insurance
- Tax residency can change; DTAs protect against double taxation in most destinations
- Malaysia is the most popular destination (proximity + cost); Thailand offers best healthcare value; Australia is preferred when family ties exist
- Minimum monthly budget for a couple ranges from ~S$1,600 (Malaysia) to ~S$8,000 (Melbourne)

## 1. CPF & Financial Implications

### 1.1 CPF LIFE Payouts Overseas

**YES — CPF LIFE payouts and lump sum withdrawals are available regardless of your country of residence.**

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **CPF LIFE Monthly Payouts** | Can be credited to a Singapore bank account. You can then transfer overseas via remittance. Direct crediting to foreign bank accounts is generally NOT supported — check with CPF Board for exceptions. |
| **Withdrawal of CPF savings** | At age 55 (with Full Retirement Sum met) and from 65 onwards, you can withdraw savings above the Basic Retirement Sum from your Ordinary and Special Accounts, even while overseas. |
| **Medisave usage overseas** | SEVERELY RESTRICTED. Only for emergency hospitalization at CPF-approved overseas hospitals (very limited list). Non-emergency treatment overseas CANNOT use Medisave. |
| **CPF Nominations** | Can be made/updated online via SingPass regardless of location. Critical for ensuring CPF savings go to intended beneficiaries. |
| **CPF interest rates** | Continue to accrue (OA: 2.5% floor, SA/MA/RA: 4% floor) regardless of your residence. No penalty for living overseas. |
| **CPF top-ups** | You can still top up your own or family members' CPF accounts for tax relief (if you remain a Singapore tax resident) or for retirement planning. |

### 1.2 Singapore Bank Accounts

**Maintain your Singapore accounts.** They remain fully functional. Key considerations:

- **Multi-currency accounts:** DBS Multiplier, UOB Mighty FX, OCBC Global Savings allow holding foreign currencies
- **Remittance:** Wise, Instarem, and DBS Remit offer competitive rates. Wise typically cheapest for amounts under S$10,000
- **Credit cards:** Keep at least one Singapore card active for online purchases and travel
- **SingPass:** Maintain your Singapore mobile number or set up SingPass Face Verification for continued access to government e-services

### 1.3 Supplementary Retirement Scheme (SRS)

- SRS withdrawals before the statutory retirement age (currently 63) incur a 5% penalty
- After the statutory retirement age, only 50% of the withdrawal is taxable if you remain a Singapore tax resident
- **If you are no longer a Singapore tax resident at withdrawal:** the full withdrawal amount may be subject to withholding tax (15% for non-residents) UNLESS a DTA provides relief
- **Strategy:** Time SRS withdrawals carefully — ideally after establishing non-resident status in a DTA country that taxes pensions favorably

## 2. Tax Implications

### 2.1 Singapore Tax Residency

| Residency Status | Criteria | Tax Scope |
|-----------------|----------|-----------|
| **Tax Resident** | In Singapore ≥183 days in a calendar year, OR ordinarily resident | All Singapore-sourced income; foreign income remitted to Singapore |
| **Non-Resident** | In Singapore <183 days | Singapore-sourced income only (no remittance basis; employment income taxed at 15% or resident rates, whichever higher) |

**Key point:** If you live overseas full-time but still own Singapore property, rental income remains taxable in Singapore whether you're resident or not.

### 2.2 Income Tax Treatment

| Income Type | Taxable if Non-Resident? | Notes |
|-------------|--------------------------|-------|
| **Singapore rental income** | YES | 15% withholding tax for non-residents on gross rent (vs. resident progressive rates on net rent after expenses). File for resident rate if beneficial. |
| **CPF LIFE payouts** | NO | Tax-free, regardless of residence |
| **SRS withdrawals** | YES (partial) | 15% withholding tax for non-residents (or DTA rate) |
| **Singapore dividends** | YES (partial) | Generally tax-exempt under one-tier system; specific cases may apply |
| **Foreign-sourced income** | NOT taxable in Singapore | Singapore does NOT tax foreign income of non-residents |

### 2.3 Property Tax

- **Owner-occupied:** 0-16% (2024 rates, progressive) — you LOSE this concession if you're overseas and not physically occupying
- **Non-owner-occupied:** 11-27% (2024 rates) — much higher. You MUST inform IRAS if you move overseas and rent out or leave vacant.
- **Annual Value** is reassessed regardless of your tenant situation

### 2.4 Double Taxation Agreements (DTAs)

Singapore has DTAs with **100+ countries** including:
- Malaysia, Thailand, Australia, Japan, Portugal, Spain, New Zealand, Vietnam

DTAs typically ensure you don't pay tax twice on the same income. For retirees, the most relevant DTA provisions cover:
- **Pensions and annuities** — which country has taxing rights
- **Real property income** — always taxable where the property is
- **Capital gains** — often only taxable in the country of residence

**Warning — Taiwan:** No DTA with Singapore. Income could be taxed in both jurisdictions.

### 2.5 Tax Residency in Destination Country

This is the OTHER side. Once you're a tax resident of your retirement country, that country may tax:
- Your worldwide income (including CPF LIFE, SRS withdrawals, SG rental income)
- Your worldwide assets (wealth taxes in Spain, Netherlands)
- Inheritance/gifts

**Critical:** Always consult a cross-border tax specialist before committing to a move.

## 3. Healthcare Abroad

### 3.1 MediShield Life Overseas Coverage

| Scenario | Covered? | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|
| **Emergency hospitalization** | YES (limited) | Claims assessed case-by-case, pegged to B2/C ward rates in Singapore public hospital. Expect significant out-of-pocket shortfall. |
| **Non-emergency treatment** | NO | Planned procedures, chronic care excluded |
| **Outpatient treatment** | NO | GP visits, specialist consultations, medication excluded |
| **Pre-authorization** | Not required | But notification to CPF Board within reasonable time after emergency is advisable |

### 3.2 Integrated Shield Plans (IPs)

Private hospital insurance riders. Overseas coverage varies dramatically:

| Insurer/Plan | Overseas Coverage | Geographic Zone |
|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **AIA HealthShield Gold Max A** | Emergency only overseas | Worldwide emergency |
| **Prudential PRUShield Premier** | Limited overseas | Emergency + planned treatment at panel hospitals |
| **Great Eastern SupremeHealth P Plus** | Emergency overseas | Worldwide |
| **Raffles Shield** | Emergency + planned | Panel hospitals in Malaysia, Thailand |

**Key limitation:** Even top-tier IPs cover overseas treatment only at panel hospitals and typically emergency only. For living overseas, an IP is NOT adequate — you need international health insurance.

### 3.3 International Health Insurance Options

| Provider | Annual Premium (Couple, Age 60) | Coverage |
|----------|-------------------------------|----------|
| **Cigna Global** | S$8,000–15,000 | Worldwide incl. USA; inpatient + outpatient |
| **Bupa Global** | S$10,000–20,000 | Comprehensive; direct billing network |
| **Allianz Care** | S$5,000–12,000 | Good value; worldwide excl. USA option |
| **Aetna International** | S$7,000–14,000 | Strong Asia coverage |
| **April International** | S$4,000–10,000 | Budget-friendly; good for SE Asia only |

**Cost-saving strategies:**
- Choose "Worldwide excluding USA" to cut premiums by 40-50%
- Increase deductible/excess (S$2,500–5,000)
- Consider local private insurance if staying in one country (e.g., Malaysia AIA, Thailand Bupa)
- Some countries (Portugal, Spain, Taiwan, Japan) allow foreigners with residency to join public healthcare systems

### 3.4 Returning for Singapore Healthcare

Many retirees maintain the option to return for serious medical care:
- **Flights:** Budget S$500–1,500 return from SE Asia; S$1,500–3,000 from Europe/Australia
- **Subsidized care:** Returning Singaporeans can access subsidized care at public hospitals (B2/C wards) — unlike MediShield Life, there is NO waiting period for citizens returning from overseas
- **Private care:** Full access at private hospitals, paying out-of-pocket or via IP
- **Strategy:** Some retirees buy a basic IP for Singapore use + local insurance for day-to-day overseas care

## 4. Visa & Residency Options

### 4.1 Malaysia — MM2H (Malaysia My 2nd Home)

| Tier | Fixed Deposit (RM) | Monthly Income (RM) | Annual Visa Fee (RM) |
|------|-------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Silver** | RM 150,000 (≈S$45,000) | RM 7,000 (≈S$2,100) | RM 500 |
| **Gold** | RM 500,000 (≈S$150,000) | RM 10,000 (≈S$3,000) | RM 1,000 |
| **Platinum** | RM 1,000,000 (≈S$300,000) | RM 15,000 (≈S$4,500) | RM 2,000 |

- **Duration:** 5 years, renewable
- **Path to PR:** Possible but very difficult
- **Pros:** Close to Singapore, English widely spoken, food identical
- **Cons:** Fixed deposit locked for 1 year; after that, 50% can be withdrawn for approved expenses (property, education, medical)

### 4.2 Thailand — Retirement Visa (Non-Immigrant O-A)

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| **Age** | 50+ |
| **Financial** | 800,000 THB (≈S$30,000) deposit in Thai bank, OR 65,000 THB/month income (≈S$2,500), OR combination |
| **Health insurance** | Mandatory — 400,000 THB inpatient / 40,000 THB outpatient |
| **Criminal record** | Clean required |
| **Duration** | 1 year, renewable annually |

**Alternative: Thailand Elite Visa**
- 5-year option: 900,000 THB (≈S$34,000)
- 10-year option: 1,500,000 THB (≈S$56,000)
- No financial proof needed beyond visa fee; no 90-day reporting

**Long-Term Resident (LTR) Visa — Wealthy Pensioner category:**
- Age 50+, income ≥80,000 USD/year, investment ≥250,000 USD in Thai assets
- 10-year visa, tax benefits, no 90-day reporting

### 4.3 Australia — Parent Visa Categories

| Visa Type | Cost (AUD) | Wait Time | Requirements |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| **Contributory Parent (143)** | ~AUD 50,000 (≈S$45,000) | **12+ years** | Balance of family test (≥50% children in Australia) |
| **Contributory Parent (Temp 173)** | ~AUD 33,000 | 12+ years | 2-year temp, then upgrade to 143 |
| **Non-Contributory (103)** | ~AUD 6,500 | **30+ years** | Lower priority |
| **Aged Parent (804)** | ~AUD 6,500 | 30+ years | Must be in Australia when applying; age 67+ |

**Key issue:** The wait times make Australia impractical for most retirees unless you apply early and wait.

### 4.4 Portugal — D7 Passive Income Visa

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| **Minimum income** | ≈€820/month (Portuguese minimum wage) — though in practice, €1,200–1,500/month recommended |
| **Proof of accommodation** | 12-month rental contract or property ownership |
| **Criminal record** | Clean |
| **Health insurance** | Required for first year |
| **Duration** | 2 years initially, then 3 years renewal. Path to citizenship after 5 years. |

**Tax note:** NHR (Non-Habitual Resident) regime partially phased out. Consult tax advisor on latest rules.

### 4.5 Other Destinations — Quick Reference

| Country | Visa Type | Key Requirement | Path to PR/Citizenship |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|------------------------|
| **Japan** | Designated Activities (Long Stay) | Age 55+, ¥30M (≈S$300K) savings, private health insurance | Possible after 5-10 years |
| **Spain** | Non-Lucrative Visa | €28,800/year income, health insurance | After 5 years residency |
| **New Zealand** | Parent Retirement Visa | NZ$1M investment + NZ$500K settlement funds | After 4-5 years |
| **Vietnam** | No proper retirement visa | 3-month e-visa (visa runs common but unreliable) | Not available |
| **Taiwan** | No retirement visa | Investment-based residency (NT$6M+ ≈ S$270K), or resident via work/study then switch | After 5 years residency |
| **UK** | No retirement visa | Standard Visitor 6 months, or Innovator/Investor visas | Very difficult |
| **USA** | No retirement path | EB-5 investment ($800K+), family-sponsored | Complex |

## 5. Legal & Estate Planning

### 5.1 Wills

**You should have:**
1. **Singapore will** — for Singapore assets (property, CPF, bank accounts, SRS)
2. **Will in retirement country** — for assets acquired there (property, local bank accounts, investments)

**Cross-border issues:**
- Some countries (e.g., most EU countries) recognize the concept of "forced heirship" — you cannot freely distribute your estate
- A Singapore will alone may not be valid for foreign property
- Probate in multiple jurisdictions is expensive and slow

### 5.2 Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA)

- A Singapore LPA is **NOT automatically valid overseas**. Some countries accept it with an apostille/legalization; most require a local equivalent.
- **Recommendation:** Execute an LPA in both Singapore AND your retirement country.

### 5.3 HDB Rules for Overseas Owners

| Rule | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Minimum Occupation Period (MOP)** | Must be met BEFORE you can sublet the whole flat |
| **Subletting while overseas** | Allowed after MOP, but must get HDB approval and renew every 3 years. You cannot leave it vacant long-term. |
| **Non-owner-occupied property tax** | Applies immediately — significantly higher rates |
| **Maintenance** | You remain responsible for HDB-mandated upgrading costs |

### 5.4 Inheritance & Estate Taxes

- **Singapore:** No estate duty (abolished 2008)
- **Malaysia:** No inheritance tax
- **Thailand:** No inheritance tax for non-resident foreigners
- **Japan:** Inheritance tax up to 55% for residents (high!)
- **Australia:** No inheritance tax
- **Portugal:** No inheritance tax for direct family
- **Spain:** Inheritance tax varies by region (Andalusia exempts up to €1M for children)
- **UK:** 40% above £325K threshold

## 7. Decision Framework

### 7.1 Which Destination Matches Your Priorities?

| Priority | Best Destination(s) |
|----------|---------------------|
| **Lowest cost** | JB: S$1,600/month — 70% cheaper than Singapore |
| **Best healthcare** | Bangkok (Bumrungrad) or Japan (universal) |
| **Closest to family in SG** | JB (30 min drive) or KL (1 hr flight) |
| **Family in Australia** | Perth/Melbourne — only practical if family can sponsor |
| **European lifestyle** | Portugal D7 visa — most accessible EU path |
| **No language barrier** | Malaysia, Australia, NZ — English native |
| **Safest environment** | Japan, Singapore, Taiwan (all top 5 globally) |
| **Four seasons climate** | Japan, Portugal, Spain, Australia (south), NZ |
| **Asian food** | Malaysia — identical to Singapore food culture |

### 7.3 Pre-Move Checklist

1. **CPF:** Verify CPF LIFE payout start date. Set up SG bank account for crediting. Make CPF nomination.
2. **Tax:** Consult cross-border tax specialist. Understand DTA provisions. Plan SRS withdrawal timing.
3. **Healthcare:** Review MediShield Life limitations. Research and purchase international/local health insurance. Identify English-speaking hospitals near destination.
4. **Visa:** Confirm eligibility requirements. Prepare financial proof. Budget for visa fees and deposits.
5. **Property:** Decide whether to sell, rent, or keep SG property. Factor in non-owner-occupied property tax if keeping. Check HDB subletting rules.
6. **Legal:** Execute wills in both Singapore and retirement country. Create LPA in both jurisdictions. Update CPF/insurance nominations.
7. **Banking:** Open multi-currency account. Set up recurring remittance path. Keep SG credit card active.
8. **SingPass:** Ensure mobile number or Face Verification works from overseas. Test access before leaving.
9. **Community:** Join Singaporean/Malaysian diaspora Facebook groups at destination. Connect with existing expat communities.
10. **Trial:** Before permanent move, do a 1–3 month trial stay to validate assumptions about lifestyle, costs, and healthcare access.
