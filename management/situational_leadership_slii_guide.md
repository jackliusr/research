## 1. What is SLII?

Situational Leadership® II (SLII) is a leadership framework developed by Ken Blanchard's team that teaches leaders to adapt their style — the balance of **directive** and **supportive** behaviour — to the **development level** of the person they are leading on a **specific task or goal**. The core premise: **there is no single best leadership style.** Effective leadership depends on correctly diagnosing where a follower stands on a task and applying the style that matches that diagnosis.

**Key tenets:**
- **Task-specific, not person-wide:** A developer might be D4 (self-reliant) on front-end work but D1 (enthusiastic beginner) on cloud infrastructure.
- **Dynamic, not static:** Development levels shift as people learn, face challenges, or take on new responsibilities.
- **Developmental intent:** The ultimate goal is to move followers along the development continuum toward self-reliance.

---

## 2. Origin and Evolution

**Hersey-Blanchard Situational Leadership Theory (1969):** Paul Hersey and Ken Blanchard introduced the original theory in *Management of Organizational Behavior*. Drawing from Reddin's 3-D Management Style Theory, they argued effective leadership is a function of the leader's style and follower "readiness" (ability + willingness). The original had four quadrants: Telling, Selling, Participating, Delegating.

**Evolution into SLII:** In the 1980s–1990s, The Ken Blanchard Companies refined the theory into **SLII**:
1. **Renamed styles:** Telling → Directing (S1), Selling → Coaching (S2), Participating → Supporting (S3).
2. **Refined follower levels:** "Readiness" became "development level" with precise D1–D4 descriptors.
3. **Added practical tools:** Diagnostic frameworks, goal-setting protocols, communication patterns.
4. **Built training infrastructure:** Certification programs, SLII Experience™, digital tools.

SLII is now one of the most widely taught leadership models globally, used by thousands of organisations including Microsoft, American Express, Nike, and Intel.

## 3. The Core Model — Two Axes

SLII maps leadership behaviour along two independent axes:

**Axis 1: Directive Behaviour (Task Focus)** — Clearly telling people what to do, how to do it, when, and providing close supervision. Includes goal-setting, defining roles, specifying methods, establishing deadlines, monitoring progress.

**Axis 2: Supportive Behaviour (Relationship Focus)** — Providing encouragement, asking for input, explaining the "why," building confidence, facilitating two-way communication. Includes active listening, praise, collaborative problem-solving, emotional support.

Crossing these two axes creates four leadership styles:

| | Low Supportive | High Supportive |
|---|---|---|
| **High Directive** | S1 — Directing | S2 — Coaching |
| **Low Directive** | S4 — Delegating | S3 — Supporting |

## 4. The Four Leadership Styles (S1–S4)

### S1 — Directing (High Directive, Low Supportive)
The leader defines roles, provides specific instructions, and supervises closely. Communication is primarily one-way: the leader tells the follower what, how, when, and why. **Use when:** The follower is new to a task — low competence, high commitment (D1). **Risk of overuse:** Stifles growth. **Risk of underuse:** Leaves beginners confused and anxious.

### S2 — Coaching (High Directive, High Supportive)
The leader continues providing direction while also explaining decisions, soliciting input, offering encouragement, and building confidence. Communication is two-way — the leader explains the "why" behind the "what." **Use when:** The follower has developing competence but low commitment — the disillusioned learner (D2). **Risk of overuse:** Creates dependency. **Risk of underuse:** Leaves struggling learners without needed support.

### S3 — Supporting (Low Directive, High Supportive)
The leader steps back from directing and focuses on facilitating, listening, encouraging, and collaborating. The leader acts as a sounding board — asking questions and drawing out ideas. **Use when:** The follower is capable (high competence) but needs confidence-building or validation (D3). **Risk of overuse:** Fails to push capable people to full autonomy. **Risk of underuse:** Prematurely delegating leads to mistakes.

### S4 — Delegating (Low Directive, Low Supportive)
The leader turns over day-to-day decision-making and execution to the follower, remaining available but not directing or actively coaching. **Use when:** The follower is both competent and committed — a self-reliant achiever (D4). **Risk of overuse:** Abandoning those who still need guidance. **Risk of underuse:** Micromanaging high performers, causing disengagement.

## 5. Follower Development Levels (D1–D4)

Development level is a combination of **competence** (knowledge, skills, experience) and **commitment** (motivation, confidence) relative to a **specific task or goal**.

### D1 — Enthusiastic Beginner
| Dimension | Level |
|-----------|-------|
| **Competence** | Low — new to the task, lacks knowledge and skills. |
| **Commitment** | High — excited, eager, motivated by the opportunity. |
| **Mindset** | "I'm excited! Tell me what to do." |
| **Need** | Clear direction, structured guidance, close supervision. |

### D2 — Disillusioned Learner
| Dimension | Level |
|-----------|-------|
| **Competence** | Low to developing — has gained some knowledge but far from proficient. |
| **Commitment** | Low — discouraged, frustrated, overwhelmed. Initial enthusiasm has faded. |
| **Mindset** | "This is harder than I thought. I'm not sure I can do this." |
| **Need** | Direction to build skills AND support to rebuild confidence. |

### D3 — Capable but Cautious
| Dimension | Level |
|-----------|-------|
| **Competence** | High to moderate — has the skills and experience. |
| **Commitment** | Variable — lacks confidence, hesitant, or inconsistent. |
| **Mindset** | "I know how to do this, but I'm worried about making a mistake." |
| **Need** | Active listening, encouragement, collaborative validation. |

### D4 — Self-Reliant Achiever
| Dimension | Level |
|-----------|-------|
| **Competence** | High — proven mastery of the task. |
| **Commitment** | High — confident, motivated, self-directed. |
| **Mindset** | "I've got this. I'll come to you if needed." |
| **Need** | Autonomy, trust, empowerment. |

## 6. Matching Style to Level — The Matrix Diagram

```
                    SUPPORTIVE BEHAVIOUR
                  Low  ←──────────────→  High
              ┌────────────────────────────────────┐
         High │          │   S2 (D2)   │ S3 (D3)   │
              │          │  COACHING   │SUPPORTING  │
   DIRECTIVE  │          │  High Dir   │ Low Dir    │
   BEHAVIOUR  │          │  High Supp  │ High Supp  │
              ├──────────┼─────────────┼────────────┤
              │          │             │            │
         Low  │          │  S1 (D1)    │ S4 (D4)    │
              │          │ DIRECTING   │DELEGATING  │
              │          │ High Dir    │ Low Dir    │
              │          │ Low Supp    │ Low Supp   │
              └──────────┴─────────────┴────────────┘
```

### Matching Table

| Level | Matched Style | Rationale |
|:---:|:---:|---|
| **D1** — Enthusiastic Beginner | **S1 — Directing** | Build competence with clear instruction. High commitment means they're receptive to direction. No need for heavy support yet. |
| **D2** — Disillusioned Learner | **S2 — Coaching** | Continue direction (building competence) plus add significant supportive behaviour to rebuild commitment. Most hands-on style. |
| **D3** — Capable but Cautious | **S3 — Supporting** | Competence is there, so directive behaviour drops. Focus on supportive behaviour — facilitate, listen, collaborate. Ask "What do you think?" |
| **D4** — Self-Reliant Achiever | **S4 — Delegating** | Both competence and commitment are high. Minimal direction and support needed. Turn over responsibility and let them run. |

### Common Mismatches and Consequences

| Mistake | Outcome |
|---------|---------|
| S1 with D3/D4 — micromanaging capable people | Resentment, disengagement, turnover |
| S4 with D1/D2 — abandoning beginners | Anxiety, failure, blame |
| S3 with D1 — support without direction | Confusion — follower doesn't know what to do |
| S2 with D4 — over-coaching a self-reliant achiever | Frustration — "I already know this" |
| Staying in S2 too long | Dependency — follower never learns autonomy |
| Skipping S1→S2→S3→S4 progression | Follower struggles, regresses |

## 7. Diagnosing Development Level

Accurate diagnosis is the most critical skill in SLII. A wrong diagnosis leads to a mismatched style and poor outcomes.

### The Diagnostic Framework

Ask two questions about the follower **on a specific task or goal**:

1. **Competence:** Does this person have the knowledge, skills, and experience to perform this task without guidance?
   - *Low:* Needs step-by-step instruction and close supervision.
   - *Developing:* Has some experience but far from proficient.
   - *High:* Has demonstrated capability on similar tasks.

2. **Commitment:** How motivated and confident is this person about this task?
   - *High:* Enthusiastic, confident, intrinsically motivated.
   - *Low:* Frustrated, discouraged, doubtful.
   - *Variable:* Inconsistent — confident one day, hesitant the next.

### Diagnostic Tips
- **Observe behaviour, not just words.** A follower may say "I'm fine" while their output suggests otherwise.
- **Ask probing questions.** "How confident are you about this approach?" "What part feels hardest?"
- **Review work product.** Competence shows in output — quality, decision-making, error rates.
- **Track patterns over time.** One bad week isn't regression; sustained decline is.
- **Remember: it's task-specific.** Same person can be D4 on reporting but D2 on presentations.

### Common Diagnostic Errors

| Error | Why It Happens | How to Avoid |
|-------|---------------|-------------|
| **Halo Effect** | Excelling at one task → assumed competent at all tasks. | Diagnose per task. |
| **Recency Bias** | Recent success/failure colours assessment. | Look at the trend, not the last data point. |
| **Personality Confusion** | Quiet = D2, enthusiastic = D1. | Separate competence from confidence. |
| **Competence/Commitment Confusion** | Low output → low commitment, but real issue is low skill. | Check: do they need skills or motivation — or both? |

## 8. Goal Setting in SLII

SLII is **goal-driven**. Before diagnosing development level, the leader and follower must agree on what success looks like.

### SMART Goals in SLII
- **S**pecific — Clear and unambiguous.
- **M**easurable — Quantifiable or with observable success criteria.
- **A**chievable — Realistic given constraints.
- **R**elevant — Aligned with team and organisational objectives.
- **T**ime-bound — Has a deadline or defined review period.

### Goal Alignment Protocol
1. **Define the goal together** — leader proposes, follower refines, both agree.
2. **Define success criteria** — "What does done look like?"
3. **Agree on check-in cadence** — daily for D1, weekly for D2/D3, monthly/on-demand for D4.
4. **Document the agreement** — written goals reduce ambiguity.

Without a clear goal, you cannot diagnose development level. Goal setting is step zero of SLII.

## 9. Communication Patterns Per Style

| Style | Communication | Key Phrases |
|-------|---------------|-------------|
| **S1 — Directing** | **TELL.** One-way, explicit. Little request for input. | "Here is what I need you to do…", "The deadline is…", "Let me show you step by step." |
| **S2 — Coaching** | **SELL / EXPLAIN.** Two-way but leader-guided. Explains reasoning, asks for questions, encourages. | "Here's why we're doing it this way…", "What questions do you have?", "You're making good progress." |
| **S3 — Supporting** | **COLLABORATE.** Follower-centred. Asks for ideas, facilitates discussion, draws out thinking. | "What do you think we should do?", "How would you approach this?", "What support do you need?" |
| **S4 — Delegating** | **EMPOWER.** Follower-initiated. Remains available but doesn't initiate task conversations. | "Let me know what you need.", "You've got this — keep me posted.", "I trust your judgment." |

## 10. SLII in Practice

### 10.1 One-on-One Conversations

| Level | 1:1 Focus | Agenda |
|:---:|---|---|
| **D1** | Instruction and modelling | Review task steps, demonstrate, assign small deliverables, verify understanding. |
| **D2** | Teaching and encouragement | Review progress, troubleshoot blockers, explain rationale, reinforce wins, address doubts. |
| **D3** | Facilitation and validation | Explore options together, affirm analysis, co-create decisions. |
| **D4** | Strategic alignment | Brief check-in, remove systemic blockers, discuss career growth. |

### 10.2 Delegating Effectively

Progressive delegation framework:
- **S1 Delegation:** "Do exactly this, report back when done."
- **S2 Delegation:** "Here's what needs to happen and why. Let's discuss your plan."
- **S3 Delegation:** "Here's the outcome I need. You figure out how. Let me know if you need input."
- **S4 Delegation:** "You own this. Keep me informed at regular check-ins."

**The delegation conversation should include:** the goal and success criteria, your diagnosis of their D-level, the style you'll use, the support structure (check-in cadence, escalation path, resources), and mutual agreement.

### 10.3 Giving Feedback Based on Development Level

| Level | Feedback Approach |
|-------|------------------|
| **D1** | Frequent, specific, corrective. Focus on execution. |
| **D2** | Balanced and explanatory. Acknowledge struggle, tie to growth, pair correction with support. |
| **D3** | Collaborative and affirming. "How do you think that went?" before offering assessment. |
| **D4** | Minimal, strategic. Focus on systemic impact and leadership growth. |

### 10.4 Coaching Conversations

- **S1 Coaching:** Directive — "Watch me, you try, I'll give feedback."
- **S2 Coaching:** Guided — "Let's work through this together."
- **S3 Coaching:** Facilitative — "Walk me through your thinking. Where are you stuck?"
- **S4 Coaching:** Advisory — "What would you like from me? A sounding board? A resource?"

## 11. SLII for Tech and Engineering Leaders

Technology teams present unique challenges: fast learning curves, varied technical domains, and individualistic work. Task-specific diagnosis is especially valuable.

### Mapping Engineering Levels to D1–D4

| Engineering Role | Typical D-Level | Why | Recommended Style |
|-----------------|:---:|---|---|
| **Junior Developer** (0–18 mo) | **D1** | New to codebase, language, domain. Needs clear tasks, defined scope, instructional PR reviews. | **S1:** Assign well-scoped tickets. Provide instructional code reviews. |
| **Mid-Level Developer** (1.5–4 yr) | **D2** | Competent in basics but hitting complexity — distributed systems, architecture trade-offs. May feel stuck. | **S2:** Pair on complex work. Explain design decisions. Give stretch assignments with support. |
| **Senior Developer** (4–8 yr) | **D3** | Technically strong but may lack confidence in cross-team decisions or influencing without authority. | **S3:** Assign ownership of a service. Ask "What do you recommend?" Validate their judgment. |
| **Staff/Principal Engineer** (8+ yr) | **D4** | Expert. Drives strategy, mentors others, makes autonomous decisions. | **S4:** Give a charter, not a task list. Be available for strategic counsel only. |

**Caveat:** Never apply rigidly by title. A senior new to your tech stack is D1 on that stack. Diagnose the task, not the role.

### Adapting for Remote and Hybrid Teams

| Challenge | SLII Adaptation |
|-----------|----------------|
| Reduced visibility into behaviour | Increase intentional check-ins. Use async status updates as diagnostic data. |
| Harder to provide spontaneous coaching | Schedule coaching explicitly. Use pair programming (VS Code Live Share) for S1/S2. |
| D4 followers thrive remotely | Avoid over-checking. Use async communication (Loom, docs) for S3 support. |
| D2 followers can hide and regress | Watch for reduced communication, missed deadlines. Schedule 1:1 video calls for coaching. |
| Documentation as directive tool | Runbooks and ADRs (architecture decision records) serve as scalable S1/S2 direction. |
| Cultural and time-zone diversity | What looks like D2 disengagement may be cultural deference or time-zone lag. Ask, don't assume. |

### SLII in Engineering Practices

| Practice | SLII Application |
|----------|-----------------|
| **Code reviews** | D1: Instructional, line-by-line. D2: Explanatory — "here's why this pattern." D3: Collaborative — "what trade-offs?" D4: Lightweight. |
| **Sprint planning** | D1: Assign specific stories. D2: Discuss approach before assigning. D3: Ask for proposed stories. D4: Self-defined sprint goals. |
| **Incident response** | D1/D2: S1 — runbooks, defined roles. D3/D4: S4 — "You own this incident." |
| **On-call rotations** | D1: Pair with senior. D4: Solo rotation, mentor others. |
| **Architecture decisions** | D1/D2: Decision made by seniors, explained. D3: Collaborative decision. D4: Follower decides, informs. |

## 12. SLII vs Other Leadership Models

### vs Servant Leadership
- **SLII:** Matching style to follower development level. Leader as diagnostician and style-adaptor.
- **Servant Leadership:** Serving followers' needs as the leader's first priority. Leader as steward.
- **Key difference:** SLII is a *how-to* framework; Servant Leadership is a *philosophy*. You can apply SLII techniques within a servant-leadership philosophy.

### vs Transformational Leadership
- **SLII:** Task-level adaptation of behaviour. Develops individuals task by task.
- **Transformational:** Vision-driven inspiration and organisational change. Leader as visionary change agent.
- **Key difference:** SLII provides day-to-day operational leadership; Transformational provides strategic, inspirational context. They are complementary.

### vs Managerial Grid (Blake-Mouton)
- **SLII:** Directive ↔ Supportive axes. No single ideal style — depends on follower D-level. Follower variable is central.
- **Managerial Grid:** Concern for People ↔ Concern for Production. "Team management" (9,9) is ideal — high concern for both. Follower variable is marginal.

### vs Team Leadership (Hill's Model)
- **SLII:** Individual follower-task dyad. Focus on one-on-one style adaptation.
- **Team Leadership:** The team as a whole. Focus on team performance and external environment.

### vs Leadership Agility (Joiner)
- **SLII:** Situational flexibility in style. Tells leaders *what to do* in a situation.
- **Leadership Agility:** Developmental stages of the leader's own capacity. Explains *why* some leaders can apply situational flexibility and others can't.

## 13. Criticisms and Limitations

### 13.1 Model Oversimplification
- Reducing leadership to two axes and four quadrants may oversimplify the rich, multidimensional reality of human interaction.
- Development levels are presented as distinct categories, but development is a continuum.
- The model captures a moment in time but doesn't fully account for contextual factors like team dynamics, organisational culture, or external pressures.

### 13.2 Assumption Leaders Can Diagnose Accurately
- Studies show self-assessment and other-assessment diverge significantly.
- Leaders are subject to halo effects, confirmation bias, and attribution errors.
- Reliable diagnosis requires practice, calibration, and feedback that most organisations don't provide systematically.

### 13.3 Self-Fulfilling Prophecy
- **Pygmalion effect:** A leader's expectation (diagnosis) may produce the expected outcome — was it the style match or the expectation?
- **Golem effect:** Labeling someone D2 may cause them to perform accordingly.
- **Label stickiness:** Once labelled, perceptions are slower to update than reality.

### 13.4 Cultural Biases
- **Assertiveness norms:** High-directive style assumes a cultural context where direct instruction is acceptable. In high-power-distance cultures (e.g., many Asian cultures) direction is expected; in Nordic cultures it may be perceived as authoritarian.
- **Supportiveness norms:** High-supportive behaviour may be seen as paternalistic in individualistic cultures.
- **Commitment expression:** "Low commitment" (D2) may be misdiagnosed in cultures where deference, silence, or indirect communication is the norm.
- **Western-centric validation:** Most SLII research originates in Western contexts. Cross-cultural validation is limited.

**Mitigation:** Calibrate diagnosis against cultural norms. Ask directly rather than infer. Adapt the *expression* of directive/supportive behaviour to cultural context while preserving the underlying principle.

### 13.5 Other Limitations
- **Neglects systemic factors:** Leadership outcomes also depend on organisational systems, reward structures, team dynamics.
- **Resource constraints:** The model assumes leaders have time to diagnose and adapt for each direct report on each task. Span of control often makes this infeasible.
- **Follower preference ignored:** The model prescribes what the follower *needs* but doesn't account for what they *prefer*.
- **Limited empirical validation:** Despite widespread adoption, rigorous support for SLII's core predictions is mixed.

## 15. Practical Tips for Applying SLII

### 1. Diagnose Before You Decide
Never choose a style before diagnosing the development level. The most common SLII failure is jumping to a preferred style (e.g., "I'm a coach" → always uses S2) without checking what the follower actually needs. Review recent output, ask directly about confidence and challenges, and check your own biases.

### 2. Apply at Task Level, Not Person Level
The same person may be D4 on API development, D2 on stakeholder presentations, and D1 on a new programming language. Do not treat development level as a fixed personality trait. Re-diagnose when the task changes.

### 3. Be Flexible — Move Between Styles
A single 1:1 may start with S2 (reviewing a blocker), shift to S3 (exploring the follower's solution), and end with S1 (clarifying next deliverable). Flexibility within a conversation matters as much as flexibility across conversations.

### 4. Use Regular Check-ins to Re-Diagnose
- **D1/D2:** Re-diagnose weekly (fast change possible).
- **D3:** Re-diagnose bi-weekly or monthly.
- **D4:** Re-diagnose quarterly (unless task scope changes).

### 5. Progress Followers to Higher Development
The goal is to develop followers toward D4. Move S1→S2 as competence develops, S2→S3 as confidence returns, S3→S4 when consistent ownership emerges. Celebrate every forward step.

### 6. Don't Skip Levels
Jumping S1→S4 bypasses essential middle stages. Without S2 (coaching), the follower never learns the *why*. Without S3 (supporting), they never build independent decision-making muscle. Skipping levels produces fragile development.

### 7. Contract for the Style
Tell the follower what you're doing and why: *"For this new project, I'll give detailed direction at first and check in daily. Once you're comfortable, we'll shift to a more collaborative approach. Does that sound good?"* This builds trust and turns SLII from something done *to* the follower into something done *with* them.

### 8. Watch for Regression Triggers
Even capable followers may regress under: new team/manager/domain, organisational change, personal stress, or a significant failure. When you see regression, **re-diagnose from scratch** — don't assume the old D-level still holds.

### 9. Use SLII for Peer and Stakeholder Relationships
The diagnostic framework works laterally: a peer new to a cross-functional initiative (D1) may appreciate structured guidance; a stakeholder cautious about a proposal (D3) may need collaborative support.

### 10. Pair SLII with Other Tools
- **Radical Candor** (Scott) — For feedback delivery.
- **The Coaching Habit** (Bungay Stanier) — For S3 facilitative questions.
- **Five Dysfunctions of a Team** (Lencioni) — For team-level diagnosis.
- **Nonviolent Communication** (Rosenberg) — For supportive communication in S2/S3.

### Leadership Style Quick Reference

| | S1 — Directing | S2 — Coaching | S3 — Supporting | S4 — Delegating |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Directive Behaviour** | High | High | Low | Low |
| **Supportive Behaviour** | Low | High | High | Low |
| **Communication** | Tell | Sell/Explain | Collaborate | Empower/Trust |
| **Decision Making** | Leader decides | Leader decides, explains | Follower decides w/ input | Follower decides |
| **Check-in Cadence** | Daily | 2–3×/week | Weekly | On demand |

### Quick Diagnosis Checklist

Before every 1:1 or delegation conversation:
- [ ] What specific task/goal are we discussing?
- [ ] What is their competence level? (Low / Developing / High)
- [ ] What is their commitment level? (High / Low / Variable)
- [ ] What development level does this map to? (D1 / D2 / D3 / D4)
- [ ] What leadership style matches? (S1 / S2 / S3 / S4)
- [ ] How will I communicate this style shift?
- [ ] What is my re-diagnosis date?
