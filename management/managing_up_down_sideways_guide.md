## 1. What Managing Up, Down, and Sideways Means

### 1.1 The Three-Directional Leader

Traditional management focuses on one direction: **down** — leading your team. But in modern flat, matrix, cross-functional organizations, effective leaders operate simultaneously across **three directions**:

| Direction | Focus | Key Question |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| **Up** | Your manager and senior leaders | "How do I make my boss more effective while advancing my own impact?" |
| **Down** | Your direct reports and team | "How do I develop, empower, and inspire the people who report to me?" |
| **Sideways** | Peers, stakeholders, cross-functional partners | "How do I influence and align with people I do not manage?" |

**Managing up** is building a productive, trust-based relationship with your manager — understanding their priorities, communication style, pressures, and decision-making patterns; proactively managing expectations; and communicating in ways that make your manager more effective. It is not sycophancy or politics — it is a professional skill that transforms a transactional oversight relationship into a strategic partnership.

**Managing down** is leading, developing, and empowering your direct reports — creating psychological safety, providing clear direction, giving honest feedback, delegating with intention, and advocating for your team's careers. It is about creating the conditions for others to do their best work.

**Managing sideways** (lateral leadership) is influencing peers and stakeholders without formal authority — building cross-functional alignment, negotiating priorities with teams that have different goals, navigating organizational politics constructively, and building coalitions to drive initiatives beyond your reporting line.

## 2. Why It Matters for Career Success

### 2.1 Your Reputation Is Shaped by All Three Directions

- **Your manager** evaluates you on upward management: Do you anticipate needs? Make their job easier?
- **Your team** evaluates you on downward management: Are you clear, fair, supportive?
- **Your peers** evaluate you on sideways management: Are you reliable, collaborative?

A leader strong in only one direction develops a skewed reputation. Strong up without down looks like careerism. Strong sideways without up means you are liked but invisible. Strong down without up means your team loves you but you never get resources.

### 2.2 Promotion Depends on All Three

| Factor | Weight (Approximate) | Source |
|--------|---------------------|--------|
| Upward visibility | ~30% | Your manager and their peers |
| Team results & development | ~30% | Your team's output and growth |
| Peer trust & cross-functional impact | ~25% | Stakeholder feedback |
| Technical competence | ~15% | Individual contributions |

Leaders who neglect upward management produce results no one sees. Those who neglect downward management burn out their teams. Those who neglect sideways management become bottlenecks others avoid.

### 2.3 Effectiveness Requires Influence Beyond Your Reporting Line

Most high-impact work crosses boundaries — platform migrations touch engineering, ops, security, product, and business; product launches require marketing, sales, legal, and finance alignment. Your ability to **achieve outcomes without authority** is the defining competency of senior leadership.

### 2.4 Organizational Impact Comes from Horizontal Collaboration

The most valuable work lives in the white spaces between org charts. Leaders who master all three directions become **integration points** — connecting silos, translating between functions, and driving collective progress.

## 3. Managing Up

### 3.1 Understand Your Manager

**Their goals — what keeps them up at night:**
- What are their KPIs? What does success look like this quarter/year?
- What does their manager ask of them?
- What would make their performance review a success or failure?

**Their communication style:**
- **Detail-oriented vs. big picture:** Do they want every edge case or just the headline?
- **Written vs. verbal:** Email, Slack, or a 5-minute call?
- **Frequency preference:** Daily standups? Weekly summaries? Async-only?
- **Preferred channel:** Teams/Slack, email, in-person, shared doc?

**Their pressures:**
- What does *their* boss demand? What organizational constraints frustrate them? (Budget freezes, headcount limits, compliance, legacy systems?)
- What political challenges do they navigate?

**Their decision-making style:**
- **Data-driven:** Needs numbers and comparative analysis.
- **Consensus-seeking:** Wants stakeholder buy-in first.
- **Gut-feel:** Quick intuitive calls; may resent over-analysis.
- **Delegate-and-trust:** Wants you to decide and inform them.

**How to gather this:** Ask directly in 1:1s: "What does success look like for you this quarter?" / "How do you prefer to hear about problems?" Observe how they communicate with peers — do they ask detailed follow-up questions or move quickly to conclusions? What questions do they ask in meetings? What information do they request repeatedly? Read their calendar — what meetings dominate their time, and what does that tell you about their priorities? Ask their former reports or long-tenured team members (carefully, without prying). Keep a mental model of their pressures and update it as the organization changes.

**The manager-employee partnership mindset:**
The most effective frame for managing up is the **partnership mindset**. You and your manager are partners in achieving outcomes, not supervisor and subordinate. Your job is to make your manager more effective — and in doing so, you become more valuable. When you treat the relationship as a partnership:
- You take ownership of communication instead of waiting to be asked.
- You proactively calibrate to their style rather than expecting them to adapt to yours.
- You invest in their success because it enables yours.
- You have honest conversations about what you need without fear of seeming demanding.

### 3.2 Be Proactive, Not Reactive

- **Anticipate needs:** Prepare the quarterly data deck two weeks early. Draft briefing materials before the executive presentation.
- **Surface problems early with proposed solutions:** Never bring a problem without at least one recommended fix. Frame as: "Here is the issue. Here are three options. I recommend option 2. Shall I proceed?"
- **Provide regular status updates without being asked:** Establish a cadence — Friday afternoon updates, milestone check-ins, a running doc they can browse.
- **Flag risks before they become issues:** "I see a risk that X could happen. Here is my mitigation plan. Here is what triggers escalation."

### 3.3 Communicate Effectively

- **Tailor to their style:** Big-picture person — lead with the conclusion. Detail-oriented — lead with summary, have data in appendix. Verbal — schedule 5-minute calls. Written — send structured docs.
- **Use the Pyramid Principle:** Start with the answer, then support with grouped arguments. ❌ "We looked at three vendors..." ✅ "I recommend Vendor A for three reasons: feature completeness, security compliance, and TCO."
- **Deliver bad news early:** No surprises. Tell them as soon as you know, explain the cause factually, present the mitigation plan, ask for input on the plan.
- **Ask clarifying questions:** "I want to confirm your priority — are we slipping the feature to fix the security issue, or doing both in parallel?"

### 3.4 Build Trust

- **Deliver on commitments consistently:** Trust is built in drops, lost in buckets. Every kept commitment adds to your balance.
- **Be reliable and predictable:** Consistent quality, predictable response times, honest estimates.
- **Admit mistakes quickly:** Own it → explain impact → present fix → state what you learned. Nothing erodes trust faster than deflection.
- **Protect their time and reputation:** Do not escalate trivia. When representing them, be their proxy. Treat confidential information as sacred.

### 3.5 Manage Expectations

- **Under-promise and over-deliver:** "Two weeks" delivered in 10 days = hero. "One week" delivered in 10 days = unreliable. Same math, completely different perception.
- **Clarify scope and timeline upfront:** What is being delivered? By when? Definition of done? Explicitly out of scope? Dependencies?
- **Communicate trade-offs explicitly:** "I can take this on. Here is what I deprioritize. Which trade-off do you prefer?"
- **Set boundaries respectfully:** "I cannot make 5 PM — I have a team 1:1. Can we move it?" "Earliest I can deliver is Thursday. Does that work, or should we reprioritize?"

### 3.6 Reading the Organizational Chart

The formal org chart shows reporting lines. The **informal org chart** shows how work gets done:
- **Informal power structures:** Who does your manager consult before deciding? Which peers carry disproportionate influence (tenure, expertise, relationships)?
- **Key influencers:** People whose endorsements carry weight without formal authority. Long-tenured individuals with institutional memory.
- **Who your manager trusts:** Leverage those relationships for cross-functional work; be cautious about aligning with those your manager distrusts.
- **Organizational politics constructively:** Politics is the exercise of influence in groups. Observe before judging, stay constructive, use awareness to advance organizational goals — not personal grievances.

### 3.7 Common Mistakes — Managing Up

| Mistake | Why It Hurts | Better Approach |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Avoiding your manager | You become invisible and unpredictable | Schedule check-ins even when nothing is urgent |
| Only sharing good news | Lose credibility; manager stops trusting your updates | Share bad news early with mitigation plans |
| Questions without proposed answers | You appear reactive and dependent | Always lead with a recommendation |
| Not understanding their priorities | Work on wrong things; look misaligned | Ask explicitly: "What are your top 3 priorities?" |
| Over-committing | Pattern of missed deadlines | Negotiate trade-offs; learn to say no |
| Gossiping about your manager | Destroys trust permanently | Address concerns directly with them |

## 4. Managing Down

### 4.1 Leadership Fundamentals

**Set clear expectations:**
- **Define success criteria:** "Done means deployed to production, meets Y response time, passed security review."
- **Clarify priorities:** "This is the most important thing this week. These three matter but are secondary. Everything else can wait."
- **Establish decision rights:** "You can decide up to $5K without me. Decisions affecting other teams need my approval."

**Provide regular feedback — timely, specific, actionable:**
- **Timely:** Feedback two weeks late feels like an ambush.
- **Specific:** "Your executive summary connected technical decisions to business outcomes" — not "great presentation."
- **Actionable:** "In our next meeting, share your analysis on option B within the first 10 minutes."

**Create psychological safety:** The belief that you will not be punished for speaking up — the #1 driver of team learning and performance (per Google's Project Aristotle). It is the foundation upon which all other management practices depend.

How to build it:
- **Model vulnerability:** Admit your own mistakes publicly. Say "I was wrong" and "I do not know." When a senior leader admits uncertainty, it gives everyone permission to do the same.
- **Invite dissent explicitly:** "I have shared my view. Who sees this differently? What am I missing?" Do not just say "feedback is welcome" — actively ask for challenge.
- **Respond appreciatively to bad news:** When someone raises a concern, thank them. Do not shoot the messenger — even when the message is inconvenient. Your response to bad news determines whether you will hear about the next problem early or only after it explodes.
- **Normalize failure and learning:** When experiments fail or projects go wrong, debrief on learning, not blame. Frame it: "What did we learn? What will we do differently next time?" Create rituals like blameless post-mortems and "failure fridays" where teams share mistakes and lessons.
- **Separate intent from impact:** When someone makes a mistake, assume good intent. Focus on the impact and the fix, not on assigning fault.

Signs of low psychological safety on your team: People stay silent in meetings, issues surface late, mistakes are hidden, no one challenges ideas, turnover is high among your best people.

**Delegate effectively:** Match task to development level (see SLII in §4.7). Explain *why* not just *what*. Provide resources and air cover. Check progress without micromanaging.

### 4.2 Develop Your Team

- **Identify growth areas:** Observe what energizes vs. drains each person. Ask: "What skills do you want to build? Where do you feel stretched?"
- **Create stretch assignments:** Tasks just beyond current capability — junior leading a small feature, mid-level presenting to senior leadership, senior mentoring a new hire.
- **Provide coaching, not just telling:** Ask: "What do you think is the right approach? What have you tried? What would help?"
- **Invest in skill building:** Recommend courses, conferences, pair work, side projects. Allocate budget visibly.
- **Advocate for career progression:** Champion promotions, recommend for high-visibility projects, make introductions. If their growth exceeds your team, help them find the right move.

### 4.3 Communication

- **Regular 1:1s — 30 min weekly, their agenda, focus on them:** Never cancel. Start with "What is on your mind?" Rotate through: roadblocks, career development, feedback (both ways), well-being, organizational context. This is NOT a project update meeting.
- **Team meetings — alignment, celebration, learning:** Keep ratio positive — for every problem, spend equal time on progress and celebration.
- **Written communication:** Weekly team update: what we achieved, what we are working on, key decisions, blockers. Share context from leadership: "Here is what I learned that affects us."

### 4.4 Build Trust

- **Be consistent and fair:** Same standards for everyone. Do not create favorites. Be predictable — your team should not guess whether it is a "good day" to raise concerns.
- **Follow through on commitments:** If you promise an introduction or a budget fight, do it. If you cannot, communicate proactively.
- **Protect your team from organizational noise:** Filter what reaches them. Shield them from unreasonable demands. Translate organizational changes into team impact.
- **Admit when you are wrong:** "I made the wrong call. Here is why. Here is what I am changing."
- **Show vulnerability appropriately:** "I do not have all the answers." "I made a mistake." "I need your help." Vulnerability builds trust — emotional dumping undermines confidence.

### 4.5 Performance Management

- **Address issues early:** A small pattern unaddressed for months becomes entrenched.
- **Use specific behavioral feedback:** Focus on observable behavior, not personality: "You interrupted the PM three times" not "bad attitude."
- **Document patterns:** Date, what happened, what was discussed. For clarity, not punishment.
- **Clear improvement plans:** Define gap, specify what must change, set timeline and check-ins, provide support, define consequences.
- **Difficult conversations with compassion:** Be direct and kind. Focus on impact, not intent. Listen. End with clarity: "Going forward, here is what needs to be different."

### 4.6 Motivation

Every person is motivated differently. Understand individual drivers:

| Driver | Description | How to Address |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| **Autonomy** | Control over work and approach | Give ownership of outcomes; allow flexible schedules |
| **Mastery** | Getting better at meaningful work | Provide learning, challenges, coaching |
| **Purpose** | Work that matters | Connect tasks to customer impact and company mission |
| **Recognition** | Being seen and valued | Public praise, shout-outs, visibility with leaders |
| **Compensation** | Fair pay and advancement | Advocate for raises, be transparent about comp philosophy |

**Tailor recognition:** Ask "How do you prefer to receive recognition?" Some love public praise; others prefer a quiet thank-you. Honor the answer. Celebrate wins publicly. Learn from failures without blame — blameless post-mortems focused on system, not person.

### 4.7 Delegation — The SLII Model

Ken Blanchard's SLII model matches delegation style to development level on a *specific task*:

| Level | State | Competence | Commitment | Best Style |
|-------|-------|-----------|------------|------------|
| **D1** | Enthusiastic Beginner | Low | High | **S1 Directing** — tell them what, how, when |
| **D2** | Disillusioned Learner | Low-moderate | Low | **S2 Coaching** — explain, guide, encourage, check often |
| **D3** | Capable but Cautious | Moderate-high | Variable | **S3 Supporting** — let them do it, be available, encourage |
| **D4** | Self-Reliant Achiever | High | High | **S4 Delegating** — hand over fully, check infrequently |

**Practical steps for any delegation:**
1. Define success criteria: "What does done look like?"
2. Provide necessary authority: "You can decide X. Escalate Y."
3. Agree on check-in points at specific intervals.
4. Resist reversing their decisions — unless critical, let it stand.

### 4.8 Common Mistakes — Managing Down

| Mistake | Why It Hurts | Better Approach |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Micromanaging | Destroys autonomy; you become a bottleneck; best people leave | Delegate outcomes, use SLII, trust until broken |
| Not giving feedback | People feel invisible; don't know what to continue | Real-time specific feedback; 5:1 positive:constructive ratio |
| Unclear expectations | Team works hard on wrong things | Write down success criteria, priorities, decision rights |
| Inconsistent behavior | Team manages your moods instead of working | Pause before reacting; separate person from problem |
| Taking credit for team work | Team stops going above and beyond; they leave | Give credit publicly, take blame privately |
| Not protecting the team | Team absorbs every disruption and burns out | Filter, buffer, translate. Shield them. |

## 5. Managing Sideways

### 5.1 Influence Without Authority

Getting things done with people who do not report to you and have their own priorities.

- **Build relationships before you need them:** Coffee chats when there is no ask. Offer help before requesting it. Attend cross-functional meetings even when not required.
- **Understand peers' goals and pressures:** Frame requests in terms of *their* success. "My proposal protects your uptime metrics." "This reduces your Q4 workload."
- **Find mutual benefit:** Visibility, help with their priorities, reduced future work, solving a shared problem. Frame everything around what the other person gains.
- **Be reliable and helpful:** Every interaction builds or depletes your reputation. Hit cross-team deliverables. Give your best thinking freely. Acknowledge and reciprocate help.
- **Build reputation for competence and collaboration:** Share knowledge generously. Admit what you do not know. Connect people. Be easy to work with.

### 5.2 Cross-Functional Collaboration

- **Identify shared goals:** Find the superordinate goal that serves both parties and anchor every negotiation around it.
- **Understand different functional languages:**

| Function | Values | Language | Priority |
|----------|--------|----------|----------|
| Engineering | Simplicity, scalability | Architecture, latency, tech debt | Long-term system health |
| Product | User value, speed | User stories, roadmaps, OKRs | Time-to-value |
| Business | Revenue, risk, compliance | P&L, ROI, SLA | Bottom line |
| Operations | Stability, reliability | Runbooks, incident response, change mgmt | Uptime |

Translate: "We need to refactor the monolith" → "We must invest in stability to prevent outages costing X in revenue."

- **Create alignment around customer/user value** — the universal language.
- **Establish shared metrics** all parties can support.
- **Document agreements and decisions:** Write down what was decided, who agreed to what, timeline, escalation path. Circulate and ask for confirmation.

### 5.3 Networking Internally

Internal networking is often misunderstood as self-promotion or careerist glad-handing. In reality, it is **building a web of relationships that make work faster, smarter, and more collaborative**. When you need to unblock a project, understand a policy change, or get early buy-in for an initiative, your network determines whether you get the answer in 10 minutes or 10 days.

- **Build a diverse network:** Across functions (engineering, product, design, business, legal, finance, HR, ops), levels (ICs, managers, directors, VPs), tenures (new hires bring fresh perspective, long-tenured people hold institutional memory), and teams (not just your division). A homogenous network gives you narrow information; a diverse network gives you the full picture.
- **Be genuinely helpful:** Share information others might find useful. Make introductions between people who should know each other. Offer to review a doc, give feedback on a proposal, or help debug a cross-team problem. When you learn something valuable, pass it along with a note: "Thought you might find this useful."
- **Maintain relationships even when you do not need anything:** The real value of a network shows up in moments of crisis — and by then it is too late to build it. Maintain through: occasional check-ins ("How is your project going?"), sharing relevant articles, sending congratulations on wins or promotions, showing up for events and talks.
- **Be visible:** Volunteer to present at all-hands. Contribute thoughtful questions in cross-functional meetings. Write internal blog posts or brown-bag materials. Teach a lunch-and-learn on a topic you know well. Visibility is not self-promotion — it is making your expertise available to others.

### 5.4 Navigating Conflict

- **Focus on interests, not positions (Getting to Yes):** Positions are "delay the release two weeks." Interests are "we fear the system will crash." Probe for the *why* behind the position and generate solutions that address it without conceding your position.
- **Separate people from problems:** "Sarah is blocking this" → "There is a disagreement about the rollout timeline." Treat the problem, not the person, as the opponent.
- **Generate options for mutual gain:** Phased rollouts, feature flags, resource sharing, timeline adjustments with conditional triggers.
- **Use objective criteria:** Industry benchmarks, company policy, pilot data, precedent.
- **Know when to escalate:** When options exhausted, authority exceeded, relationship damaged, or strategy/values at stake. Escalate constructively: "We agree on the problem but disagree on approach. We need your perspective to break the tie."

### 5.5 Stakeholder Management

**Identify key stakeholders** and map them on the Power/Interest Grid:

| | Low Interest | High Interest |
|---|-------------|--------------|
| **High Power** | **Keep Satisfied** — Monitor, high-level updates | **Manage Closely** — Full engagement, frequent updates |
| **Low Power** | **Monitor** — Minimal effort | **Keep Informed** — Regular updates, no deep engagement |

**Tailor engagement:** High-power/high-interest → weekly updates, involve in decisions. High-power/low-interest → monthly briefings, escalate only major decisions. Communicate proactively — do not wait to be asked. Tailor messages to each stakeholder's concerns.

### 5.6 Building Alliances

- **Find peers with shared interests:** Other managers frustrated by the same problems. Product leads wanting more research. Operations leads tired of the same approval bottlenecks.
- **Build coalitions for change:** 2–3 peers with aligned goals present a united front. Align on message, support each other, share credit.
- **Support others' initiatives:** Attend their kick-offs, offer contributions, give public endorsements.
- **Create reciprocity:** Help without being asked. Share credit. Make introductions. Defend allies in their absence.
- **Be known as someone who gets things done across boundaries:** The person everyone goes to when something needs to cross silos. Deliver reliably. Communicate proactively. Solve problems in the white spaces.

### 5.7 Organizational Awareness

Organizational awareness — often called "organizational intelligence" — is the ability to read and navigate the human dynamics of your organization. It is the difference between being surprised by decisions and anticipating them.

- **Read the room:** Beyond the words being spoken, pay attention to who speaks and who stays silent, whose opinions visibly carry weight, what topics cause energy shifts (excitement, tension, avoidance), what is *not* being said (the topics everyone tiptoes around), and non-verbal cues — posture shifts, eye contact avoidance, tone changes.
- **Understand decision-making processes:** Before every important decision, know: Who makes it? Who must be consulted? What information drives it? What is the timeline? Can it be appealed, and by whom? Decisions made by committee look different from decisions made by a single executive; align your influencing strategy accordingly.
- **Know who influences whom:** The org chart shows who reports to whom. Influence maps show who impacts decisions. Whose advice does the decision-maker seek? Which junior person has the ear of a senior leader? Which teams have earned trust through past success? Which stakeholders have veto power — formally or informally?
- **Recognize what is said vs. unsaid:** Topics that arise in hallways but not in meetings. Decisions made before the meeting and rubber-stamped during it. Feedback given through omission or tone. Conflicts managed through avoidance rather than resolution.
- **Understand organizational culture and norms:** Every organization has unwritten rules about how decisions get made (top-down vs. consensus), how conflict is handled (direct vs. indirect), how credit is given (individual vs. team), how status is shown (titles vs. tenure vs. expertise), and how change happens (slow consensus vs. fast executive order). Learn these norms to operate effectively within them while working to improve them.

### 5.8 Common Mistakes — Managing Sideways

| Mistake | Why It Hurts | Better Approach |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Only engaging when you need something | Reputation as a taker; people avoid you | Build relationships continuously; help first |
| Burning bridges | Organizations are smaller than they seem | Disagree respectfully; leave relationships intact |
| Ignoring organizational politics | Blindsided by decisions you don't understand | Understand dynamics without becoming cynical |
| No network until you need it | Trust takes months to build | Start from day one in any new role |
| Taking sides unnecessarily | Alienate half the participants | Stay neutral; focus on interests, not factions |
| Over-collaborating | Analysis paralysis; nothing gets done | Balance collaboration with decision speed |
| Failing to document agreements | Verbal agreements evaporate | Write decisions down; circulate for confirmation |

## 6. Managing in a Matrix Organization

Matrix organizations — where employees report to both a functional manager and a project/product manager — create unique challenges for all three directions of management. Matrix structures are common in consulting, technology, banking, and large engineering organizations, and they require deliberate navigation.

**Dual reporting lines:** Your direct reports may have a "dotted line" to another manager. You may yourself report to multiple leaders.
- Clarify who owns what: performance reviews, compensation decisions, day-to-day task assignment, career development. Without this clarity, team members get conflicting signals and become paralyzed.
- Coordinate with the other manager regularly — schedule a monthly sync. Do not let your report become the messenger between two bosses who do not communicate. Nothing frustrates a team member more than being the bridge between two managers who refuse to talk.
- If priorities conflict, escalate to the shared leadership above both of you rather than putting your report in the middle. Frame it: "We have a priority conflict. Can you help us decide which takes precedence?"

**Navigating multiple bosses (if you have them):**
- Establish clear ownership: who decides on what? Who approves time off? Who sets technical direction vs. delivery schedule?
- Communicate transparently: let both managers know when priorities conflict. Send a brief note: "I have a conflict between Project A (you asked for X) and Project B (they asked for Y). Which takes priority?"
- Create shared visibility: one status update that serves both stakeholders is more efficient than separate updates.
- If conflicts persist, request a **joint alignment session** with both managers — mediated by you. Come with the conflict clearly framed and proposed options.

**Building alignment across functions:** In a matrix, your team may be distributed across functions that do not naturally align:
- Create shared artifacts: project charters, RACI matrices (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed), communication plans.
- Invest time in team-building across functional boundaries — shared successes build shared identity.
- Establish shared rituals: joint standups, cross-functional retros, shared dashboards.

**Managing conflicting priorities:** Matrix structures surface tension between functional excellence (long-term skill building) and project delivery (short-term output):
- Negotiate explicit trade-offs with both sides. "If we invest in the training program, we deliver the feature two weeks later. Is that acceptable?"
- Protect your team from whipsaw — when priorities shift weekly, productivity collapses and morale follows.
- Build buffers into plans: reserve 10–20% of capacity for unplanned matrix work. Communicate this buffer explicitly so it is not mistaken for slack.

**Staying visible to multiple stakeholders:** In a matrix, your work is distributed across audiences:
- Send periodic summaries to all relevant stakeholders — do not assume they share information.
- Present results in forums where both sides of the matrix are represented.
- Gather feedback from multiple sources — do not rely on a single manager for your performance assessment.
- If you feel invisible to one side of the matrix, schedule a recurring 15-minute check-in to close the gap.

## 7. Managing in Remote/Hybrid Environments

Remote work changes tactics — the fundamentals stay the same.

- **Over-communicate intentionally:** In an office, context is absorbed passively. Remotely, nothing is. State the obvious. Over-invite to channels. Over-document decisions, rationale, status.
- **Written documentation is critical:** Use shared docs for strategy and decisions. Write meeting notes with action items. Keep decision logs current. Default to async written communication over synchronous meetings.
- **Create virtual water cooler moments:** 5 minutes of non-work chat at meeting starts. Non-work Slack channels. Optional virtual coffees. Rotating "virtual walk" pairs.
- **Schedule 1:1s rigorously:** Weekly, video, non-negotiable. Increase frequency during high-stress periods.
- **Build visibility through async updates:** Weekly written updates to manager and stakeholders. Short video updates for milestones. Thoughtful contributions to async channels.
- **Build trust without hallway conversations:** Trust builds from reliability, responsiveness, and clarity. Be exceptionally reliable. Be transparent about availability. Show care through intentional check-ins.

**Common remote pitfalls:**

| Pitfall | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Async overload | Establish norms: meeting vs. message vs. doc |
| Out of sight, out of mind | Ensure remote members present in reviews and get stretch assignments |
| Meeting fatigue | Default to async; meetings for decisions, alignment, connection only |
| Informal network gap | Schedule deliberate cross-functional introductions and virtual coffees |

## 8. Putting It All Together

Managing up, down, and sideways is a **daily practice** that compounds over time.

### 8.1 Daily Practices

- **Start the day checking your manager's priorities:** What matters most to them today? Align your work.
- **Protect your team from distractions:** Scan incoming requests — does the team need this, or can you handle it?
- **Reach out to a peer:** One message of help, thanks, or connection. Keep relationships warm.
- **End the day with a brief note:** What did you accomplish? Who needs follow-up?

### 8.2 Weekly Practices

- **Prepare for your manager 1:1:** Outcomes you want, support you need, what they should know.
- **Hold team 1:1s:** 30 min each, their agenda, not project status.
- **One cross-functional touchpoint:** Check in with a peer in another team. Offer help. Share context.
- **Reflect on relationships:** Any strained? Any neglected? Any feedback needed?
- **Send a Friday update:** Brief written summary to manager and stakeholders: wins, progress, risks, asks.

### 8.3 Quarterly Practices

- **Assess relationship health in all three directions:** Up — do I understand current priorities? Down — are team members engaged and growing? Sideways — are cross-functional relationships strong?
- **Gather feedback:** Ask specific input from your manager, team, and 2–3 key peers.
- **Adjust approach:** Based on feedback, identify 1–2 changes.
- **Invest in key relationships:** Schedule time with a neglected stakeholder.

### 8.4 Annual Practices

- **360-degree feedback:** Gather input from manager, peers, direct reports, other stakeholders.
- **Relationship audit:** Rate each significant working relationship — strong, adequate, needs repair, beyond repair. Plan for each.
- **Set relationship goals:** What kind of leader do you want to be seen as next year?
- **Celebrate progress:** Improvement is the goal, not perfection.

## 10. Conclusion

Managing up, down, and sideways is not a technique — it is a **mindset**. Leadership in modern organizations is systemic, not hierarchical. Your impact is determined by how effectively you connect your team to organizational priorities (up), develop the people in your care (down), and navigate the horizontal relationships that make or break every initiative (sideways).

**Three truths to remember:**
1. **Your manager is a human being** with goals, fears, and pressures. Manage up to make them more effective, and they will do the same for you.
2. **Your team is your legacy.** Leaders who develop people and create psychological safety build organizations that outlast them. Manage down to grow capabilities, not just output.
3. **Your peers are your partners.** The most impactful work lives between teams. Manage sideways to bridge gaps, align interests, and achieve what no single team can alone.

Start today. Pick one direction. Take one action. Observe the result. Adjust. Repeat.
