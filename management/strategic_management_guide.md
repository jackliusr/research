## 1. What Is Strategic Management?

Strategic management is the **ongoing, organisation-wide process** of formulating, implementing, and evaluating cross-functional decisions that enable an organisation to achieve long-term objectives. It integrates external analysis with internal capabilities to determine direction and execution.

### 1.1 Strategic Management vs Operational Management

| Dimension | Strategic Management | Operational Management |
|---|---|---|
| **Time horizon** | Long-term (3–10+ years) | Short-term (daily to annual) |
| **Focus** | Direction, positioning, competitive advantage | Efficiency, productivity, process execution |
| **Scope** | Organisation-wide, cross-functional | Departmental, functional, process-level |
| **Decision level** | Board, C-suite, senior leadership | Middle management, functional leads |
| **Uncertainty** | High — deals with VUCA environments | Low to moderate — standard operating procedures |
| **Resource commitment** | Large, irreversible, long-lived | Incremental, reversible within budget cycles |
| **Performance metric** | Market share, ROIC, competitive position | Throughput, cost per unit, SLA compliance |
| **Key question** | "Are we doing the right things?" | "Are we doing things right?" |

Operational management keeps the organisation running efficiently day-to-day; strategic management is about **choosing which game to play and how to win it**.

### 1.2 Strategic Management vs Strategic Planning

- **Strategic Planning:** a **periodic exercise** — annual/quarterly event where strategy is formalised. Analytical, structured, deliberate.
- **Strategic Management:** the **broader, ongoing process** encompassing planning but extending into implementation, monitoring, learning, and adaptation. Strategy is not an annual event — it is lived daily.

Mintzberg criticised planning as confusing analysis (its strength) with synthesis (its weakness). Planning produces plans; strategic thinking produces **strategic direction** — real strategy emerges from deliberate *and* emergent approaches.

## 2. The Strategic Management Process

The classical strategic management process is an **iterative, four-stage cycle**:

```
  Environmental Scanning
          ↓
    Strategy Formulation
          ↓
    Strategy Implementation
          ↓
   Evaluation and Control
          ↓
   (Feedback loop → Scan again)
```

### 2.1 Environmental Scanning

Scanning the external and internal environments to identify opportunities, threats, strengths, and weaknesses. Techniques include PESTLE, Porter's Five Forces, competitor analysis, internal capability audits, and stakeholder mapping.

### 2.2 Strategy Formulation

Developing the organisation's **mission, vision, values**, and the strategic choices that will guide resource allocation. This is where frameworks (SWOT, VRIO, Ansoff, BCG, Blue Ocean) are applied to determine **corporate-, business-, and functional-level strategies**.

### 2.3 Strategy Implementation

Putting the chosen strategy into action. This is widely regarded as the hardest phase — execution failures account for 60–90% of strategy failures. Implementation involves structure, culture, resource allocation, processes, people, and performance management systems (Balanced Scorecard, OKRs).

### 2.4 Evaluation and Control

Measuring results against objectives, identifying variances, conducting strategic reviews, and deciding when to persist, pivot, or exit. Feedback from evaluation feeds back into environmental scanning, creating a learning loop.

## 3. Strategy Formulation Frameworks

### 3.1 Porter's Five Forces

Developed by Michael Porter (1979). Analyses the **competitive structure of an industry** to determine its attractiveness.

| Force | Description |
|---|---|
| **Threat of New Entrants** | Barriers to entry (capital, regulation, brand loyalty, economies of scale). High barriers → lower threat. |
| **Bargaining Power of Buyers** | When buyers are concentrated, price-sensitive, or have low switching costs, they can force prices down. |
| **Bargaining Power of Suppliers** | When suppliers are concentrated or offer unique inputs, they can raise prices or reduce quality. |
| **Threat of Substitutes** | Alternative products/services from outside the industry that fulfil the same need. |
| **Competitive Rivalry** | Intensity of competition — affected by number of competitors, industry growth rate, exit barriers, product differentiation. |

**Use case:** Industry attractiveness assessment, entry/exit decisions, positioning strategy.  
**Criticism:** Static snapshot; underestimates complements and ecosystem dynamics.

### 3.2 PESTLE Analysis

Macro-environmental scanning across six dimensions:

| Dimension | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Political** | Government stability, tax policy, trade restrictions, tariffs |
| **Economic** | GDP growth, inflation, interest rates, unemployment, disposable income |
| **Social** | Demographics, cultural norms, lifestyle trends, education levels |
| **Technological** | Automation, R&D activity, digital transformation, AI/ML |
| **Legal** | Labour laws, consumer protection, health & safety, data privacy (GDPR, CCPA) |
| **Environmental** | Climate change, sustainability regulations, ESG reporting mandates |

**Use case:** Input to SWOT opportunities/threats; market entry assessment; long-range planning.

### 3.3 SWOT Analysis

| | **Helpful** | **Harmful** |
|---|---|---|
| **Internal** | **Strengths** (resources, capabilities, brand, IP) | **Weaknesses** (gaps, resource constraints, skill deficits) |
| **External** | **Opportunities** (market trends, new technology, unmet needs) | **Threats** (competitors, regulation shifts, disruption) |

**Use case:** Simple, intuitive starting point for strategy discussion.  
**Pitfall:** Subjective, often lists everything rather than prioritising what matters most.

### 3.4 VRIO Framework (Barney, 1991)

A resource-based view (RBV) tool for assessing whether internal capabilities can be a source of **sustained competitive advantage**.

| Criterion | Question | If No → |
|---|---|---|
| **Value** | Does the resource enable the firm to exploit opportunities or neutralise threats? | Competitive disadvantage |
| **Rarity** | Is the resource controlled by few competitors? | Competitive parity |
| **Imitability** | Is it costly for others to imitate or substitute? | Temporary advantage |
| **Organisation** | Is the firm organised to capture value from the resource? | Untapped potential |

**Use case:** Internal capability audit; M&A target assessment; outsourcing decisions.  
**Key insight:** Only resources that satisfy **all four** criteria can yield sustained competitive advantage.

### 3.5 Ansoff Matrix (Ansoff, 1957)

A growth strategy framework mapping **products** against **markets**:

| | **Existing Markets** | **New Markets** |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing Products** | **Market Penetration** — lower prices, more marketing, loyalty programmes | **Market Development** — new geographies, new customer segments, new channels |
| **New Products** | **Product Development** — new features, new lines, innovation | **Diversification** — new products in new markets (highest risk) |

**Use case:** Growth strategy brainstorming, risk assessment of expansion moves.

### 3.6 BCG Growth-Share Matrix (Boston Consulting Group, 1970s)

Portfolio analysis tool categorising business units / products by **market growth rate** and **relative market share**:

| Quadrant | Characteristic | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Stars** | High growth, high share | Invest heavily; build |
| **Cash Cows** | Low growth, high share | Harvest profits; minimise investment |
| **Question Marks** | High growth, low share | Selective investment or divest |
| **Dogs** | Low growth, low share | Divest, liquidate, or reposition |

**Use case:** Portfolio resource allocation; product lifecycle management.  
**Criticism:** Oversimplifies; assumes market share dominance equals profitability; ignores synergies.

### 3.7 Blue Ocean Strategy (Kim & Mauborgne, 2005)

Argues that sustained success comes from **creating uncontested market space** rather than battling in crowded "red oceans".

**Core tools:**
- **Strategy Canvas** — a visual diagnostic and action framework that captures the current competitive landscape and helps design a new value curve.
- **Four Actions Framework** — Eliminate, Reduce, Raise, Create.
- **ERRC Grid** — the practical tool for applying the four actions.

**Use case:** Innovation strategy, new market creation, differentiation.  
**Examples:** Cirque du Soleil (eliminated animals/star performers, raised artistry), Nintendo Wii (created casual gaming market).

### 3.8 Core Competence (Hamel & Prahalad, 1990)

Identifies the **collective learning** in the organisation that coordinates diverse production skills and integrates technologies.

**Three tests:**
1. **Provides potential access** to a wide variety of markets.
2. **Makes a significant contribution** to perceived customer benefits of the end product.
3. **Difficult for competitors to imitate.**

**Use case:** Diversification strategy, R&D prioritisation, make-or-buy decisions.  
**Classic example:** Honda — core competence in small engines → diversified into cars, motorcycles, lawnmowers, generators, marine engines.

### 3.9 Scenario Planning (Royal Dutch Shell / Schwartz)

A structured approach to imagining **multiple plausible futures** rather than predicting a single one. Shell famously used it to anticipate the 1973 oil crisis.

**Process:** 1. Identify focal issue → 2. Analyse driving forces → 3. Rank by importance & uncertainty → 4. Develop 3–4 distinct scenario narratives → 5. Stress-test current strategy → 6. Identify signposts.

**Use case:** Long-range planning in high-uncertainty environments; geopolitical risk; climate strategy.  
**Key distinction:** Scenarios challenge mental models — they are not forecasts.

## 4. Strategy Implementation

Execution is where most strategies fail. The best-formulated strategy is worthless if it cannot be implemented.

### 4.1 Balanced Scorecard (Kaplan & Norton, 1992)

Translates strategy into performance measures across four perspectives:

| Perspective | Example Metrics |
|---|---|
| **Financial** | ROE, Revenue growth, Cost-to-income ratio, EVA |
| **Customer** | NPS, Customer retention, Market share, CSAT |
| **Internal Process** | Cycle time, Quality rate, Operational efficiency |
| **Learning & Growth** | Employee engagement, Training hours, Innovation rate |

#### 4.1.1 Balanced Scorecard Example — Regional Bank (Crédit Agricole CIB–style)

| Perspective | Objective | KPI / Target | Initiative |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Financial** | Improve profitability | ROE > 12% | Cost transformation programme |
| Financial | Grow revenue | Net banking income +5% YoY | Cross-sell campaign |
| Financial | Optimise cost base | Cost-to-income ratio < 55% | Branch digitisation |
| **Customer** | Increase loyalty | NPS +15 pts | RM retraining |
| Customer | Grow SME segment | SME market share 18% | Dedicated SME advisory |
| Customer | Improve digital experience | Digital adoption 70% | Mobile app v3.0 |
| **Internal Process** | Accelerate loan approvals | Decision time < 48 hrs | Automated credit scoring |
| Internal Process | Strengthen risk management | NPL ratio < 2.5% | Early warning system |
| Internal Process | Improve operational efficiency | STP rate 85% | Workflow automation |
| **Learning & Growth** | Build digital capabilities | 60% staff digitally certified | Digital academy |
| Learning & Growth | Enhance leadership bench | Succession coverage 90% | HiPo programme |
| Learning & Growth | Foster innovation culture | 2 ideas/employee/year | Innovation lab |

**Strategy map linkage:** L&G (digital skills) → Internal Process (automation) → Customer (digital adoption) → Financial (cost-to-income).

### 4.2 OKRs — Objectives and Key Results (Intel / Doerr; popularised by Google)

Cascading goal-setting framework from top-level strategy down to team/individual level:

- **Objectives:** Qualitative, inspirational, time-bound.
- **Key Results:** Quantitative, measurable, verifiable outcomes. Stretch target: 60–70% attainment.

#### 4.2.1 OKR Cascade Example — Banking Organisation

**Corporate OKR (CEO)**

- **Objective:** Become the #1 digital bank for SMEs in Southeast Asia by 2028.
  - KR1: Grow SME loan portfolio from SGD 2.5B to SGD 4.0B.
  - KR2: Achieve SME NPS of 65 (current: 42).
  - KR3: Reduce SME onboarding time from 14 days to < 2 days.
  - KR4: Generate 35% of SME revenue from digital channels (current: 12%).

**↓ Retail Banking OKR (Head of Retail)**

- **Objective:** Drive SME acquisition and deepen wallet share.
  - KR1: Acquire 8,000 new SME accounts (run rate: 3,000/yr).
  - KR2: Increase cross-sell from 2.1 to 3.5 products per SME client.
  - KR3: Launch bundled "SME Starter Pack" with accounts, payments, and credit.

**↓ Digital Channels OKR (Head of Digital)**

- **Objective:** Deliver frictionless digital onboarding and lending for SMEs.
  - KR1: Build end-to-end digital SME onboarding flow (Q2).
  - KR2: Reduce loan decision time from 72 hrs to < 4 hrs via automated scoring.
  - KR3: Achieve 90% user satisfaction on new SME mobile module.
  - KR4: Integrate e-KYC with MyInfo and MyKad by Q3.

### 4.3 Hoshin Kanri (Policy Deployment)

A Japanese strategic management methodology (Toyota, Bridgestone) that ensures **everyone in the organisation is rowing in the same direction**.

**Key elements:**
- **True North** — long-term vision/philosophy (10+ year horizon).
- **Hoshin (Breakthrough Objectives)** — 1–3 year strategic priorities.
- **Catchball Process** — iterative negotiation between top-down targets and bottom-up capability assessment; goals are refined through dialogue rather than dictated.
- **X-Matrix** — one-page visual aligning long-term goals, annual objectives, process measures, and responsible teams.
- **PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Act)** — embedded review cycle at every level.

**Vs OKRs:** Both cascade goals, but Hoshin places heavier emphasis on cross-functional alignment, visual management, and the catchball consensus-building process. OKRs are simpler and more flexible for fast-moving tech organisations.

**Strategy maps** visualise cause-and-effect between objectives across the four Balanced Scorecard perspectives. Kaplan and Norton introduced them to answer: *"How do L&G investments drive Financial results?"*

**Typical chain:**
> Employee training (L&G) → Process improvement (Internal) → Faster service / better quality (Customer) → Revenue growth / cost reduction (Financial)

### 4.5 Strategic Initiatives and Portfolio Management

Strategic initiatives are the **projects and programmes** that operationalise strategy. Portfolio management ensures:

- **Strategic alignment** — every initiative maps to a strategic objective.
- **Resource balancing** — do not over-invest in one area at the expense of others.
- **Risk diversification** — mix of low-risk incremental and high-risk breakthrough initiatives.
- **Stage-gate governance** — initiatives reviewed at milestones; poor performers are killed or redirected.

### 4.6 Organisational Structure Alignment

Structure follows strategy (Chandler, 1962). Four common structural types:

| Structure | Description | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Functional** | Grouped by function (Marketing, Ops, Finance) | Single product / small organisations |
| **Divisional** | Grouped by product, geography, or customer segment | Diversified firms; clear P&L accountability |
| **Matrix** | Dual reporting (e.g., product × geography) | Complex multi-dimensional organisations |
| **Network** | Core hub + outsourced/partner capabilities | Agile, digital-first, platform businesses |

**Strategic trap:** Trying to execute a **coordination-intensive strategy** (e.g., integrated solutions) through a **siloed functional structure**.

### 4.7 Resource Allocation and Budgeting

Strategy lives where money goes. Key practices:

- **Zero-based budgeting (ZBB):** Every budget cycle starts from zero; every expense must be justified against strategy.
- **Strategic buckets:** Pre-allocate a fixed percentage of capital to innovation / growth initiatives (e.g., 70% core, 20% adjacent, 10% transformational — the "70-20-10 rule" popularised by Google).
- **Beyond Budgeting model:** Decentralise budgeting authority to enable adaptive resource allocation rather than fixed annual budgets.

### 4.8 Change Management in Strategy Execution

Strategy execution is inherently a **change process**. Major models:

| Model | Key Principle |
|---|---|
| **Kotter's 8-Step Model** | Urgency → Coalition → Vision → Communicate → Empower → Quick wins → Consolidate → Anchor |
| **ADKAR** (Prosci) | Awareness → Desire → Knowledge → Ability → Reinforcement |
| **Lewin's 3-Step** | Unfreeze → Change → Refreeze |
| **Nudge Theory** (Thaler) | Subtle environmental changes to encourage desired behaviours |

**Critical success factors:** Visible executive sponsorship, clear communication of "why", psychological safety for experimentation, early wins to build momentum, and dedicated change champions.

## 5. Strategy Evaluation

The monitoring, measurement, and corrective-action component of the strategic management cycle.

### 5.1 KPIs and Strategic Metrics

| Type | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Leading indicators** | Predict future performance | Pipeline value, employee engagement, training hours, website traffic |
| **Lagging indicators** | Measure past outcomes | Revenue, profit, market share, customer churn |
| **Health metrics** | Monitor operational stability | System uptime, compliance breaches, staff turnover |
| **Strategic metrics** | Track progress against strategic objectives | Shareholder value added, brand equity score, innovation pipeline ROI |

**Principle:** "What gets measured gets managed." But measurement must be balanced — over-indexing on lagging indicators leaves you managing in the rear-view mirror.

### 5.2 Strategic Review Cadence

| Cadence | Focus | Participants |
|---|---|---|
| **Monthly operational review** | Track KPIs, initiative status, variance | Functional leads, PMO |
| **Quarterly Business Review (QBR)** | Deep-dive against OKRs, competitive updates, pivot decisions | Executive team, business heads |
| **Annual strategy refresh** | Revisit vision, environmental scan, revise 3-year plan | Board, C-suite |
| **3–5 year strategic cycle** | Full strategic planning reset; new VP/MP if major shift | Board + extended leadership |

**Best practice:** Hold a **quarterly offsite** that is forward-looking — not a retrospective report-out. Review assumptions, surface strategic issues, and make real resource reallocation decisions.

### 5.3 Strategy Audit Tools

| Tool | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **SWOT Re-evaluation** | Check if earlier SWOT factors still hold |
| **Premise control** | Test if the assumptions underlying strategy are still valid |
| **Implementation control** | Track milestones, budgets, timelines of strategic initiatives |
| **Strategic surveillance** | Broad monitoring of external signals (press, competitor moves, regulatory changes) |
| **Special alert control** | Triggered by sudden crises or unexpected events (war, pandemic, major regulation) |

### 5.4 Variance Analysis

Compare actual performance against planned targets.

| Type | Question |
|---|---|
| **Revenue variance** | Price effect? Volume effect? Mix effect? |
| **Cost variance** | Efficiency variance? Price variance? Volume variance? |
| **Timeline variance** | On track? Delayed? Pulled forward? |

Variance analysis feeds **strategic intervention decisions**: adjust resource allocation, revise targets, change initiative scope, or pivot.

### 5.5 Competitive Benchmarking

Systematic comparison of your performance, processes, and practices against competitors or best-in-class organisations.

**Types:**
- **Competitive benchmarking** — direct competitors in same industry.
- **Functional benchmarking** — best-in-class for a specific function (regardless of industry).
- **Strategic benchmarking** — how peers have navigated similar strategic challenges.

### 5.6 Strategy Pivot Decisions

When do you abandon a strategy? Key signals:

1. **Assumption failure** — the external environment changed in a way that invalidates the strategy's foundation.
2. **Performance gap** — consistent and significant shortfall versus targets without credible recovery plan.
3. **New opportunity** — an unexpected opportunity emerges that renders the current path suboptimal.
4. **Competitive displacement** — a competitor permanently alters the market structure.
5. **Resource depletion** — the organisation cannot sustain the required investment.

Pivot is not failure — it is strategic learning (see Mintzberg's Learning School below).

## 6. Mintzberg's 10 Schools of Strategy

Mintzberg, Ahlstrand & Lampel (1998) categorised strategy literature into **10 schools** across three categories:

### 6.1 Prescriptive Schools (How strategy *should* be formulated)

| School | Core Idea | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Design School** | Strategy as a deliberate process of conception — SWOT-driven. CEO is the chief strategist. | Simple, visual (SWOT), intuitive. | Separates thinking from doing; assumes stable environment; CEO as sole strategist is unrealistic in complex orgs. |
| **2. Planning School** | Strategy as a formal, disciplined, step-by-step planning process — budgets, forecasts, scenarios. | Rigorous, systematic, scalable. | Analysis paralysis; over-emphasises formal plans; stifles creativity; planning = / = strategy. |
| **3. Positioning School** | Strategy as analytical positioning — select industries and positions using economic frameworks (Porter). | Analytical rigour; industry-focus; actionable. | Over-emphasises external analysis; neglects internal capabilities; static — strategy becomes a one-time positioning exercise. |

### 6.2 Descriptive Schools (How strategy *actually* gets made)

| School | Core Idea | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|---|
| **4. Entrepreneurial School** | Strategy as vision in the mind of the visionary leader. | Powerful for startups; fast decision-making; clear direction. | Over-reliance on a single individual; founder dependency; can become dogma. |
| **5. Cognitive School** | Strategy as a mental process — how strategists perceive, process, and frame information. | Recognises cognitive biases; connects strategy to psychology. | Difficult to operationalise; individual focus misses organisational dynamics. |
| **6. Learning School** | Strategy as an emergent process — organisations learn through experimentation and feedback. | Realistic for VUCA environments; embraces adaptation; enables innovation. | Can lack coherence; may produce directionless drift without guiding frameworks. |
| **7. Power School** | Strategy as a political process — negotiation, coalition-building, conflict among stakeholders. | Recognises organisational reality; useful for M&A and alliances. | Can over-emphasise conflict; may promote cynical or self-serving behaviour. |
| **8. Cultural School** | Strategy rooted in organisational culture, shared beliefs, and social interaction. | Emphasises collective identity; explains strategic persistence. | Can become a barrier to change; culture is hard to change deliberately. |
| **9. Environmental School** | Strategy is determined by the environment — firms position reactively or perish. | Highlights external forces; useful in highly regulated or turbulent industries. | Deterministic; leaves little room for agency; underplays internal strategic choice. |

### 6.3 Integrative School

| School | Core Idea | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|---|
| **10. Configuration School** | Strategy as episodes of transformation — organisations exist in stable configurations that periodically transform. | Integrates all other schools; explains strategic change cycles; most comprehensive. | Can feel like "everything and nothing"; complex to apply without deep context. |

**Mintzberg's verdict:** No single school is sufficient. Effective strategic management requires **judiciously combining elements** from multiple schools depending on the context. The Configuration School offers the most integrative lens.

## 7. Corporate vs Business vs Functional Strategy

Strategy operates at **three hierarchical levels**:

### 7.1 Corporate Strategy

Decides **where to compete** — which industries, markets, and businesses the organisation should be in.

| Strategy | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Diversification** | Entering new businesses (related or unrelated) | Amazon (retail → AWS → media → healthcare) |
| **Vertical Integration** | Owning more of the supply chain | Tesla building battery factories |
| **M&A** | Buying growth or capabilities | Salesforce acquiring Slack |
| **Divestiture** | Selling off non-core businesses | GE selling finance arm |
| **Restructuring** | Fundamental change in asset/operations | IBM pivot hardware → cloud/consulting |
| **Portfolio Management** | Managing business mix for balanced return | Berkshire Hathaway |

**Key frameworks:** BCG Matrix, GE McKinsey Matrix, Core Competence analysis.

### 7.2 Business Strategy

Decides **how to compete** in a specific market. Porter's three **generic strategies** remain the dominant framework:

| Strategy | Description | When It Works |
|---|---|---|
| **Cost Leadership** | Lowest-cost producer in the industry | Price-sensitive customers; standardised products; scale economies |
| **Differentiation** | Unique, superior value commanding a premium | Customers value distinct features; innovation capability; brand matters |
| **Focus** | Serving a narrow segment better than broad competitors | Niche market; poorly served by broad competitors |

**Contemporary critique:** In digital markets with platform economics, Porter's generic strategies are challenged. Amazon combines cost leadership *and* differentiation, while platform businesses win by **orchestrating ecosystems** rather than choosing a single position.

### 7.3 Functional Strategy

Decides **how each function supports** the business strategy.

| Function | Strategic Questions |
|---|---|
| **Marketing** | Which segments? What positioning? Which channels? Brand architecture? |
| **Operations** | Make or buy? Lean vs agile? Where to locate? Capacity strategy? |
| **Finance** | Capital structure? Dividend policy? Investment criteria? Treasury strategy? |
| **HR** | Talent acquisition strategy? Learning & development? Performance management? Culture? |
| **Technology / IT** | Build vs buy? Cloud strategy? Data architecture? Security posture? AI adoption roadmap? |

**Key principle:** Functional strategies must be **aligned and mutually reinforcing**.

## 8. Strategic Management in Different Contexts

### 8.1 Banking and Financial Services

| Strategic Priority | Description |
|---|---|
| **Regulatory strategy** | Navigating Basel III/IV, IFRS 9, AML/KYC, MiFID II, local regulations. Compliance as both constraint and competitive differentiator. |
| **Digital transformation strategy** | Channel shift (branch → mobile), core banking modernisation, open banking / PSD2, embedded finance. |
| **Risk management strategy** | Credit risk models, market risk limits, operational risk frameworks, stress testing, climate risk (TCFD). |
| **Balance sheet optimisation** | Capital allocation, liquidity management, funding structure, RWA optimisation. |
| **Platform / ecosystem** | Embedding banking in non-banking journeys (e-commerce, property, mobility). |

**Recent trend:** Banks moving from product-centric to **customer-centric platform models** — DBS and JPMorgan exemplify this.

### 8.2 Technology Companies

| Strategic Priority | Description |
|---|---|
| **Platform strategy** | Building multi-sided platforms with network effects (marketplaces, developer ecosystems). |
| **Product line strategy** | Managing suites, bundling, lifecycle, and versioning across products. |
| **Innovation pipeline** | Horizon planning (McKinsey's 3 Horizons): H1 (core), H2 (adjacent), H3 (transformational). |
| **Talent strategy** | Attracting and retaining engineering/AI talent in a hyper-competitive market. |
| **Monetisation model** | Subscription (SaaS), freemium, advertising, transaction fees, data licensing. |

**Key difference:** Tech firms face much shorter strategy cycles (6–12 months) compared to banking or manufacturing (3–5 years).

### 8.3 Startups vs Mature Firms

| Dimension | Startup | Mature Firm |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary strategic focus** | Product-market fit → scaling | Defending share → efficiency → renewal |
| **Strategy cycle** | Weeks to months | Quarters to years |
| **Planning approach** | Lean startup, build-measure-learn, pivots | Annual planning, portfolio reviews, M&A |
| **Risk tolerance** | High (existential bets) | Low-moderate (managed risk) |
| **Resource advantage** | Speed, focus, talent | Capital, brand, distribution, data |
| **Key strategic risk** | Running out of runway | Disruption / inertia |
| **Best-fit schools** | Entrepreneurial, Learning, Cognitive | Design, Planning, Positioning, Cultural |

### 8.4 Public Sector vs Private Sector

| Dimension | Public Sector | Private Sector |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary objective** | Public value, service delivery, policy outcomes | Shareholder value, profit, competitive advantage |
| **Stakeholders** | Citizens, politicians, regulators, taxpayers | Shareholders, customers, employees, partners |
| **Performance metric** | Outcome-based, often qualitative | Financial, market-based |
| **Strategy horizon** | Political cycle (3–5 years) plus long-term policy | Market cycle (1–10 years) |
| **Key constraint** | Budget allocation, political mandate, legislation | Competition, capital markets, customer choice |
| **Failure consequence** | Public inquiry, political fallout | Bankruptcy, takeover |

**Note:** New Public Management brought strategic management tools into government, but political context fundamentally shapes strategy.

## 9. Strategic Management Tools and Techniques

| Tool / Technique | Purpose | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Scenario Analysis** | Explore multiple plausible futures | High uncertainty; long horizons; capital-intensive decisions |
| **War Gaming** | Simulate competitive dynamics and responses | M&A defence; market entry; pricing decisions |
| **Strategy Canvas** | Visualise industry factors and design a new value curve | Blue Ocean strategy work; differentiation analysis |
| **Value Chain Analysis** | Identify where value is created/captured across the firm's activities | Cost reduction; differentiation; outsourcing decisions |
| **Stakeholder Mapping** | Identify influence, interest, and engagement strategy | Change initiatives; M&A; regulatory strategy |
| **Issue-Based Strategic Planning** | Focus strategy on the 3–5 most critical issues facing the organisation | Turnaround; crisis; resource-constrained settings |
| **Strategic Group Mapping** | Map competitors by strategic dimensions (e.g., price × breadth) | Competitive positioning; identifying strategic gaps |
| **Growth-Share Matrix (BCG)** | Portfolio resource allocation | Multi-business portfolio review |
| **Experience Curve** | Cost declines as cumulative production doubles | Pricing strategy; capacity planning; learning targets |
| **McKinsey 7S Framework** | Diagnose organisational alignment across 7 elements: Strategy, Structure, Systems, Skills, Staff, Style, Shared Values | Post-M&A integration; organisational change; strategy execution diagnosis |
| **Strategic Choice Cascade** | A sequence of 5 interlinked choices: Where to play? How to win? Capabilities? Management systems? (Lafley & Martin) | Strategy articulation; simplicity in complex organisations |
| **Good Strategy Bad Strategy (Rumsfeld)** | Good strategy = diagnosis → guiding policy → coherent action. Bad strategy = fluff, failure to face the problem, mistaking goals for strategy. | Strategy critique and diagnosis; avoiding common pitfalls |

## 10. Strategic Thinking vs Strategic Planning

Mintzberg argued forcefully that **strategic planning is not strategic thinking**. The distinction is fundamental:

| Dimension | Strategic Planning | Strategic Thinking |
|---|---|---|
| **Core process** | Analysis — break down, formalise, document | Synthesis — connect, imagine, reframe |
| **Orientation** | Convergent — narrowing down options | Divergent — generating new possibilities |
| **Strategy type** | Deliberate — intended and implemented as planned | Emergent — patterns that coalesce from actions |
| **Role of data** | Past data drives forecasts and projections | Insight from data sparks new questions |
| **Risk posture** | Risk minimisation; control | Calculated risk; exploration |
| **Output** | A strategic plan (document) | Strategic direction (shared understanding) |
| **Who does it** | Planners, analysts, finance | Leaders, entrepreneurs, frontline innovators |
| **Best environment** | Stable, predictable, mature industries | Turbulent, uncertain, high-change environments |

### The Balance

Mintzberg's position is not "planning is bad." His argument is that:

- **Strategic planning** is excellent for **programming** strategy — operationalising and controlling it.
- **Strategic thinking** is what produces real strategic insight.
- The error is conflating the two — believing that a planning process will, by itself, produce strategic insight.

**Practical synthesis:** Use strategic thinking (dialogue, debate, customer immersion, scenario exploration) to *formulate* strategic insight, then use strategic planning (budgets, roadmaps, KPIs) to *operationalise* it. The best organisations create **space for both** — they do not let the planning calendar crowd out thinking time.

## 11. Current Trends and Future Directions

### 11.1 Digital Strategy — AI, Data, and Platform Business Models

AI is no longer just an IT strategy — it is a **core strategic capability**. Key developments:

- **AI strategy** — not just adopting AI tools, but redesigning business models around AI (data network effects, AI-augmented decision-making, autonomous processes).
- **Data as a strategic asset** — data moats, data monetisation, synthetic data for privacy.
- **Platform business models** — 70% of unicorns are platform businesses; traditional firms are being forced to platformise (banking-as-a-service, industrial IoT platforms, SaaS ecosystems).

### 11.2 ESG as Strategic Imperative

Environmental, Social, and Governance factors have moved from corporate social responsibility to **core strategic concern**:

- **Net-zero commitments** reshape cost structures, supply chains, and capital access.
- **Social license to operate** becomes a competitive advantage.
- **Regulatory tsunami** — CSRD (EU), ISSB standards, SEC climate disclosure rules.
- **Green financing** — sustainable bonds and ESG-linked loans surpass conventional instruments in growth rate.

**Strategic implication:** Firms that treat ESG as a compliance burden will be outcompeted by those that see it as **innovation opportunity and risk hedge**.

### 11.3 Geopolitical Risk in Strategy

| Risk | Strategic Response |
|---|---|
| US-China decoupling | Dual supply chains; China+1 sourcing strategy |
| Trade tariffs / sanctions | Near-shoring, friend-shoring; regionalisation |
| Supply chain fragility | Inventory buffers; multi-sourcing; regional hubs |
| Talent restrictions | Relocate R&D; diversify talent pools |
| Cybersecurity / state actors | Zero-trust architecture; resilience investment |

The old assumption of a stable, benign global trading environment is gone. **Geopolitical risk management** is now a mandatory strategic competency.

### 11.4 Agility in Strategy

Traditional 5-year strategic plans are increasingly viewed as obsolete. **Adaptive strategy** approaches include:

- **Strategic flexibility** — staged commitments, real options thinking, modular investments.
- **Continuous strategy** — strategy reviewed and adjusted quarterly or even monthly, not annually.
- **Strategy as a dynamic capability** — the organisation's ability to sense, seize, and reconfigure (Teece, 2007).
- **Objective-based pivoting** — strategy sets the "what" and "why"; teams decide the "how" iteratively.

### 11.5 Ecosystem Strategy

Competition is shifting from **firm vs firm** to **ecosystem vs ecosystem**:

- **Platform ecosystems** — Apple (app devs + accessory makers), Alibaba (merchants + logistics + finance), DBS (banking + property + e-commerce).
- **Strategic partnerships** — co-opetition, joint ventures, data-sharing consortia.
- **Open innovation** — leveraging external communities for R&D.
- **API-as-product** — exposing capabilities as services to enable third-party innovation.

### 11.6 Sustainable Competitive Advantage in the AI Era

Porter's original notion of sustainable advantage (long-lasting, defensible) is challenged in a world where:

- **Scale advantages erode** — AI tools are available to small firms via APIs.
- **Speed of imitation** is faster than ever (digital products can be copied in weeks, not years).
- **Talent mobility** is high.
- **Regulation moves slowly** relative to technology.

**New sources of advantage:**
- **Data moats** — proprietary, high-quality datasets that improve AI models.
- **Learning velocity** — the ability to experiment, learn, and adapt faster than competitors.
- **Ecosystem orchestration** — being the platform others build on.
- **Trust and brand** — in an AI-mediated world, trust becomes a premium differentiator.
- **Execution culture** — strategy remains commodity; unique execution remains rare.

## 12. Criticisms and Limitations

### 12.1 Strategy as Top-Down Exercise

Traditional strategy processes are often led by the CEO and a small corporate strategy team, with limited input from frontline employees who understand customers and operations. The result: strategies that look good on paper but fail in practice.

**Antidote:** Open strategy (inclusive strategy formulation), catchball (Hoshin Kanri), and bottom-up strategy input.

### 12.2 Over-Reliance on Analytical Frameworks

Frameworks provide structure but can become cruthes:

- SWOT becomes a laundry list.
- Porter's Five Forces is static.
- BCG matrix ignores synergies.
- "Framework fatigue" — teams produce strategy documents filled with matrix templates but no real strategic insight.

**Antidote:** Use frameworks as scaffolding, not answers. Always interrogate: does this framework capture the specific dynamics at play here?

### 12.3 Strategy Inertia

Even good strategies fail because organisations can't change fast enough:

- **Structural inertia** — established processes, hierarchies, and systems resist change.
- **Cultural inertia** — "the way we do things around here" blocks new approaches.
- **Commitment escalation** — continuing to invest in a failing strategy because of sunk cost, ego, or fear of admitting error.
- **Success trap** — past success breeds complacency and resistance to strategic renewal.

**Antidote:** Build strategic disconfirmation processes — regular reviews that explicitly ask "what would cause us to abandon this strategy?"

### 12.4 Strategic Planning as Annual Ritual

In many organisations, strategic planning is a **predictable annual calendar event**: offsite → SWOT → mission statement → budget → filed away until next year. The process becomes a ritual rather than a genuine strategic dialogue.

**Symptoms:** The plan is never referenced during the year; initiatives are added but never retired; the same revenue projections appear year after year regardless of reality.

**Antidote:** Replace annual planning with a **continuous strategy dialogue** — monthly strategic topics in executive meetings, quarterly offsites with real decisions, no more than 3–5 strategic priorities at any time.

### 12.5 Difficulty of Predicting in VUCA Environments

Volatility, Uncertainty, Complexity, and Ambiguity (VUCA) make long-range planning increasingly unreliable:

- The 2020 pandemic invalidated most strategic plans overnight.
- AI disruption timelines are impossible to predict.
- Geopolitical shocks (wars, sanctions, trade blockades) are exogenous and unpredictable.

**Antidote:** Scenario planning, real options thinking, strategic flexibility, and a shift from "predict and plan" to "sense and respond."
