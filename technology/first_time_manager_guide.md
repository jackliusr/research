# Building a High-Performing Team: A First-Time Manager's Guide

A comprehensive playbook for transitioning from individual contributor to people leader —
covering the mindset shift, team building fundamentals, and practical strategies for creating
a team that delivers exceptional results. Tailored for technology and banking contexts.

---

## 1. The IC-to-Manager Transition

### The Biggest Shift

Your job is no longer about your output. It is about your team's output. As an individual
contributor (IC), you were measured on code written, designs delivered, problems solved
directly. As a manager, your craft becomes enabling others. Satisfaction shifts from "I built
that" to "they built that, and I helped make it possible."

### Common Challenges

**Letting go of technical work.** The temptation to jump into code is overwhelming. Every
time you solve a technical problem yourself, you rob a team member of growth. Reserve
technical work for emergencies only, and even then, pair with someone so they can handle it
next time.

**Imposter syndrome.** You were promoted because someone saw leadership potential. The
feeling of not knowing what you're doing is universal. The antidote is admitting what you
don't know, asking good questions, and learning fast. Your team doesn't need you to have all
the answers — they need you to help them find theirs.

**Managing former peers.** Address the change explicitly. Have a one-on-one with each
former peer: "Our relationship is changing. I'm still the same person but I have different
responsibilities. I commit to being transparent and fair. I ask you to give me a chance to learn
this job."

**Not knowing what your team does day-to-day.** You lose granular visibility. This is by design.
Trust your team's reporting, validate with data, and resist the urge to micromanage.

**Delegating for the first time.** You've been successful because you're good at your craft.
Letting someone else do it — possibly slower, possibly worse — feels wrong. But delegation
is not about getting the task done perfectly; it's about building team capability. Define the
outcome clearly, provide resources, agree on check-in points, and then let go. Let them do it
differently than you would have. The short-term quality loss is an investment in long-term
capacity.

**Dealing with performance issues.** Address issues early with data and compassion. Avoidance
punishes the whole team, including the underperformer.

### Mindset Shifts Required

| From (IC) | To (Manager) |
|---|---|
| My output matters | My team's output matters |
| I solve problems | I enable problem-solvers |
| I write code | I write culture |
| I execute | I set direction |
| I have answers | I ask questions |
| My success = my work | My success = my team's growth |

## 2. The Manager's Job Description

Five core responsibilities that everything else traces back to:

**1. Create clarity.** Goals, priorities, decision rights. Your team cannot execute if they
don't know what to do, why it matters, or who decides what. Document decision rights
explicitly using a framework like DACI (Driver, Approver, Contributor, Informed).

**2. Provide context.** People perform better when they understand *why* their work matters.
Connect daily work to the bigger picture. Over-communicate context — people need to hear
something 3-5 times before it lands.

**3. Remove obstacles.** Blockers, bureaucracy, resource constraints, organisational noise.
Ask in every 1:1: "What's slowing you down? What can I take off your plate?" Shield the
team from noise and remove barriers they cannot remove themselves.

**4. Develop people.** Coaching, growth opportunities, feedback, career planning. If you
develop your people well, the team's output takes care of itself. Your legacy is not the
product you shipped — it's the leaders you developed.

**5. Build culture.** Culture is what happens when you're not in the room. It's built by what
you model, what you reward, and what you tolerate. Psychological safety, accountability,
continuous improvement, and recognition are the pillars.

## 3. The First 90 Days as a New Manager

### Days 1–30: Listen and Learn

- Schedule 1:1s with every team member (30 min weekly). Listen 80%, talk 20%. Ask: "What
  do you enjoy? What frustrates you? What should we change?"
- Understand current projects, tech stack, recent postmortems, and retros.
- Meet stakeholders (peers, your manager, cross-functional partners).
- Identify 1-2 quick wins to build credibility.
- **Don't make big changes.** You don't yet understand the system.
- Assess team health: energy levels, body language, what people complain about.

### Days 31–60: Set Direction

- Draft team mission statement. Socialise and revise with input from team and stakeholders.
- Establish rhythms: daily standup (15 min), weekly team sync (30 min), biweekly planning
  and retro.
- Start delegating — let someone run standup, own a project, facilitate retro.
- Set expectations: your communication style, availability, decision-making approach.
- Validate priorities with your manager and align on next quarter's focus.

### Days 61–90: Build Momentum

- Deliver quick wins from month one. Make sure the team gets credit.
- Introduce regular feedback (SBI model — see Section 7). Encourage feedback to you.
- Start coaching individuals in 1:1s using GROW. Identify stretch opportunities.
- Have explicit conversations about performance expectations.
- Build relationships with peer managers.
- Run an anonymous team health survey to inform next quarter's focus.

## 4. Hiring and Team Composition

### Hire for Culture Add, Not Just Culture Fit

"Culture fit" often becomes "people like me" — leading to homogeneity and groupthink.
Hire for diversity of thought, experience, and background. A team of diverse perspectives
that collaborates well is stronger than a team of brilliant people who all think alike.

### The Hiring Process

1. **Define the role.** Write what success looks like in 6 months. Be specific about outcomes.
2. **Structured interviews.** Consistent questions, scorecards, multiple interviewers.
   Assess: technical competence, collaboration, growth mindset, and team values alignment.
3. **The bar must rise.** Every hire should raise the team's average capability. If unsure,
   it's a no. Hire slow, fire fast.

### Team Topology

- **Generalists vs. specialists.** Early-stage teams need generalists. Mature teams need
  specialists. Aim for T-shaped people: deep in one area, broad across others.
- **Senior vs. junior mix.** Target roughly 1 senior : 2 mid : 1 junior.
- **Team size.** Amazon's two-pizza team rule: 6-10 people. Dunbar's number: ~15 for
  social cohesion. Manager span of control: 5-8 direct reports ideal.

### Onboarding New Members

30-60-90 day plan: Days 1-30 (setup, pairing, small tickets, buddy system, weekly check-ins);
31-60 (own a small feature, start contributing independently); 61-90 (own a project end-to-end,
join on-call, participate in retros). Assign a buddy (not you) for every new hire.

## 5. Setting Team Direction

### Vision, Mission, Strategy

- **Mission:** Why the team exists (timeless, focused).
- **Vision:** Where you're going in 1-3 years (specific, motivating).
- **Strategy:** How you'll get there (requires saying no to almost everything else).

Co-create these with the team. An imposed vision lacks buy-in.

### OKRs and Goals

OKRs (Objectives and Key Results): qualitative objectives + quantitative key results.
Principles: cascade from company to team to individual; 3-5 objectives per quarter; ~70%
confidence at start; review monthly. Alternatively use SMART goals where OKRs feel too
abstract.

### Team Charter

A single document covering: **purpose** (why we exist), **stakeholders** (who we serve),
**boundaries** (what's in and out of scope), **ways of working** (decision-making framework,
communication norms, meeting rhythms, quality standards, definition of done). Create it with
the team, not for the team. Post it somewhere visible. Revisit and update quarterly.

### Prioritisation Frameworks

- **Value vs. Effort (2x2 matrix):** Do high-value, low-effort items first.
- **RICE:** Reach × Impact × Confidence / Effort.
- **Cost of Delay / WSJF:** Prioritise items where delay is most expensive.
- **Key principle:** Align team goals to business outcomes, not outputs. Don't measure
  "features shipped" — measure "user retention improved by 15%."

## 6. Building Team Culture

### Psychological Safety

Google's Project Aristotle found psychological safety is the #1 predictor of team effectiveness.
Signs: people speak up, admit mistakes, ask for help, challenge ideas constructively.

**How to build it:**
- **Lead with vulnerability.** Admit your mistakes first. Say "I don't know" often. Ask for
  help. Your team will mirror your behaviour. If you pretend to be perfect, they will too —
  and mistakes will be hidden rather than learned from.
- **Encourage dissenting opinions.** Actively solicit counterpoints. "I think we should go
  with approach A. Who disagrees? What am I missing?" Reward people who challenge you.
- **Celebrate learning from failures.** When something goes wrong, ask "What did we learn?"
  not "Whose fault was it?" Have blameless postmortems for incidents. Frame failures as
  tuition paid for a lesson learned.
- **Respond well to bad news.** When someone tells you about a mistake, thank them. If you
  get angry or punitive, they will never tell you again — and you'll be blindsided later.

### Accountability

High standards with support. Set clear expectations, provide resources, then hold people to
the standard. Address issues early — small issues become patterns, patterns become culture.
Give frequent, specific, kind feedback.

### Autonomy

Define the what, not the how. Resist micromanaging. Give ownership within clear boundaries
(budget, compliance, architecture standards). Delegate decisions, not just tasks.

### Recognition

Celebrate wins publicly. Recognise effort, not just outcomes. Tailor recognition to individual
preferences (public praise, private thanks, time off, learning opportunities).

### Continuous Improvement

Retrospectives with real action items. Blameless postmortems for incidents. Kaizen culture:
small improvements daily. If retros don't lead to change, people stop participating.

### Psychological Safety Assessment

Quarterly anonymous surveys, 1:1 check-ins ("Is there anything you hesitate to bring up?"),
and observing meeting participation patterns.

## 7. Developing Your Team

### Coaching Over Telling

Ask questions instead of giving answers. The answer they discover sticks longer.
Powerful questions: "What do you think?", "What options have you considered?",
"What would you do if you were me?", "What's one step you can take this week?"

### The GROW Coaching Model

- **Goal:** What do you want to achieve? (specific, time-bound)
- **Reality:** Where are you now relative to that goal?
- **Options:** What could you do to close the gap?
- **Will:** What will you actually do? When?

### Delegation: SLII Model

Situational Leadership II (SLII) matches your delegation style to the person's development level
for a specific task. The same person may be D4 on one task and D1 on another.

| Level | Person's State | Best Style | What You Do |
|---|---|---|---|
| D1 | Low competence, high enthusiasm | Directing | Clear instructions, close supervision, explicit checkpoints |
| D2 | Some competence, low confidence | Coaching | Explain decisions, ask for input, encourage effort |
| D3 | High competence, variable confidence | Supporting | Listen, facilitate, help build confidence, be available |
| D4 | High competence, high confidence | Delegating | Turn over responsibility, minimal oversight, focus on outcomes |

The most common mistake: delegating to everyone the same way. The senior engineer who
thrives on autonomy will feel micromanaged if you give them S1. The junior engineer who
needs direction will feel abandoned if you give them S4.

**Good delegation checklist:**
1. Define the outcome clearly — what does success look like?
2. Provide resources, authority, and information needed to succeed
3. Agree on check-in points and how progress will be measured
4. Clarify decision rights: "You decide and inform me" vs. "We decide together"
5. Let them do it their way — even if it's not your way
6. Let them fail safely on low-risk tasks (this is how they build judgment)

### Growth Plans

Understand each person's career aspirations (not everyone wants to be a manager). Identify
skill gaps. Create stretch assignments (10% beyond current ability). Provide learning
opportunities (training, conferences, side projects, mentoring others).

### Feedback: The SBI Model

- **Situation:** When and where? ("In yesterday's sprint planning...")
- **Behaviour:** What exactly did they do? (observable, not interpretive)
- **Impact:** What was the effect?

Principles: timely (within 24 hours), specific (observable behaviour, not personality), balanced
(5:1 positive to constructive ratio), kind and direct.

### Performance Management

Document everything — good and bad performance. Address issues the first week you see
them. PIP (Performance Improvement Plan) is a last resort, not a first step.

### Effective 1:1s

Weekly, 30 min, their agenda. Never cancel. Focus on them, not project updates.
Questions: "What's on your mind?", "What's energising you?", "What's draining you?",
"How can I support you?", "What feedback do you have for me?"

## 8. Communication Rhythms

| Cadence | Meeting | Duration | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| Daily | Standup | 15 min | Coordination, not status. What I did, what I'll do, blockers. |
| Weekly | 1:1s | 30 min each | Their agenda, coaching, feedback. Never cancel. |
| Weekly | Team sync | 30 min | Updates, wins, issues. |
| Biweekly | Planning | 60-90 min | Review priorities, assign work, estimate. |
| Biweekly | Retro | 60 min | What went well, what could be better, action items. |
| Monthly | Team social | varies | Lunch, game, whatever fits the team. |
| Monthly | Org alignment | 30 min | Broader company updates, Q&A. |
| Quarterly | OKR review | 60-90 min | Did we achieve? Set next quarter's goals. |
| Quarterly | Health survey | async | Anonymous survey, share results. |

**As needed:** Incident postmortem (blameless), design review, decision documents.

**Principles:** Over-communicate context. Use the right channel (sync for complex discussions,
async for simple info). Document decisions. Assume good intent.

## 9. Managing Performance and Difficult Situations

### Low Performer

1. **Diagnose root cause:** Skill issue (trainable?), will issue (burnout, disengagement?),
   or fit issue (misalignment with the role)?
2. **Honest conversation:** Name the gap with evidence, using SBI. "I've noticed over the
   last three sprints you've completed half the story points of peers. Help me understand."
3. **Improvement plan:** Measurable criteria, support provided, timeline (4-6 weeks),
   weekly check-ins.
4. **Follow through.** Track progress. Celebrate improvement or escalate consequences.

### Behavioural Issues

Address immediately. Be specific about behaviour and impact. Don't let it slide because the
person is a high performer — a brilliant jerk costs more than they contribute. If you wouldn't
re-hire them knowing what you know, you need to act.

### Conflict Between Team Members

Listen to both sides separately. Facilitate a joint conversation focused on interests, not
positions ("What do you need?" not "Who did what?"). Agree on behavioural expectations.
Follow up individually.

### Letting Someone Go

The hardest thing you'll do. After exhausting coaching, feedback, and improvement plans,
sometimes it's necessary for team health. Be compassionate but clear. No surprises. Respect
their dignity. Afterwards, learn from the hiring miss.

### Managing Up

Advocate for your team's needs (resources, recognition, career growth). Translate team
achievements into business impact. Protect the team from organisational noise. Celebrate
the team upward — make sure senior leadership knows who is doing great work.

### Difficult Conversations

The conversations you're most tempted to avoid are the ones that matter most. Here's a
structured approach:

**Prepare:**
- What do I want to achieve? (clear outcome)
- What's the key message I need to deliver?
- What evidence do I have? (specific examples, data, observations)
- What's the best case? What's the worst case?

**Deliver:**
- Be direct but kind. Don't cushion the message so much that it becomes unclear.
- Use "I" statements: "I noticed..." / "I observed..." / "The impact I saw was..."
- Focus on behaviour, not personality. "The report was late twice" not "You're unreliable."
- Listen to their perspective. You might learn something that changes your understanding.
- Stay calm. Breathe. Don't match their emotions if they get defensive or upset.

**Follow up:**
- Document: what was discussed, what was agreed, next steps.
- Create an action plan with clear owners and timelines.
- Schedule a follow-up check-in within 1-2 weeks.

## 10. Measuring Team Health and Performance

### Output Metrics

Delivery velocity, cycle time, deployment frequency, incident count, bug escape rate. Useful
for trends; dangerous for comparison.

### Outcome Metrics

Business impact (revenue, cost savings, customer satisfaction), adoption rate, NPS, feature
utilisation. Aligned metrics ensure the team builds things that matter.

### Health Metrics

Employee engagement score, turnover rate, psychological safety score, promotion rate,
1:1 satisfaction, retro satisfaction.

### Quarterly Team Health Survey

Anonymous, 5-10 questions. Example items: "I feel safe taking risks in this team.",
"I receive useful feedback.", "My work uses my strengths.", "I trust my teammates.",
"I understand what we're trying to achieve." Plus one open-ended: "What could your manager
do better?" Share results transparently. Celebrate improvements. Act on concerns.

### Using Metrics Wisely

Metrics are for learning, not judging. They are signals, not targets. Retrospect on processes
quarterly. Adjust rhythms based on feedback. Celebrate health improvements.

## 12. First-Time Manager Checklist

### First Week

- [ ] Schedule 1:1s with each team member (30 min weekly, recurring)
- [ ] Read current projects, roadmap, recent retros, postmortems
- [ ] Meet your manager — align on expectations and priorities
- [ ] Introduce yourself and share your approach and values
- [ ] **Listen more than you talk. Don't make big changes.**

### First Month

- [ ] Complete initial 1:1s with every team member
- [ ] Meet key stakeholders: peers, cross-functional partners, skip-level
- [ ] Identify team strengths, gaps, and pain points
- [ ] Find one quick win to build credibility
- [ ] Establish team rhythms (standup, planning, retro)
- [ ] Set up regular skip-level 1:1s with your manager
- [ ] Review current OKRs/goals — ensure business alignment

### First Quarter (90 Days)

- [ ] Define or refine team mission and vision — co-create with the team
- [ ] Set team-level OKRs aligned to organisational goals
- [ ] Establish feedback culture — give regular, specific feedback
- [ ] Conduct at least one retro with tracked action items
- [ ] Address any critical performance or behavioural issues
- [ ] Career conversation with each team member
- [ ] Build relationships with peer managers
- [ ] Start coaching individuals (GROW in 1:1s)
- [ ] Run first anonymous team health survey
- [ ] Review progress with your manager

### Ongoing

- [ ] Weekly 1:1s (never cancel, their agenda)
- [ ] Quarterly OKRs — set and review
- [ ] Continuous feedback (5:1 positive to constructive ratio)
- [ ] Team development: stretch assignments, training, mentoring
- [ ] Culture building: celebrate wins, address issues, model behaviour
- [ ] Monthly skip-level 1:1s
- [ ] Quarterly anonymous health survey
- [ ] Retrospect on your own management — what's working, what's not
- [ ] Regularly ask yourself: **Is my team growing? Are they delivering? Are they happy?**

## Final Words

> "Before you are a leader, success is all about growing yourself. When you become a leader,
> success is all about growing others." — *Jack Welch*

> "A manager's job is to create the conditions for their team to succeed. Not to do the work
> themselves. Not to take the credit. To create the conditions." — *Julie Zhuo*

You will make mistakes. You will have days where you have no idea what you're doing. You
will worry about team members, difficult conversations, and projects behind schedule. All of
this is normal. All of this means you care.

The best managers are not the ones who never struggle. They are the ones who keep learning,
keep getting better, and keep putting their team first. If you do that — genuinely, consistently —
you will build a high-performing team. Not because you were born a great manager, but
because you chose to become one.
