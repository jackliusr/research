## 1. Overview

**Peter Andreas Thiel** (b. October 11, 1967) is a German-American entrepreneur, venture capitalist, hedge fund manager, and conservative political activist. He co-founded PayPal (1998) and served as its CEO until eBay acquired it for $1.5 billion in 2002. He co-founded **Palantir Technologies** (2003), the data analytics company now central to US defence and intelligence agencies. In 2005 he launched **Founders Fund**, the premier venture firm behind Facebook, SpaceX, Airbnb, Stripe, Spotify, and dozens of other billion-dollar companies. He was the **first outside investor in Facebook** — a $500,000 bet in 2004 that returned over $1 billion and made him a billionaire by 2007. He authored **"Zero to One"** (2014), one of the most influential startup books of the decade (2.5+ million copies sold, #1 NYT bestseller, translated into 30+ languages), and taught the legendary **CS183 course at Stanford** whose class notes became the book.

**Why his talks matter:** Thiel is arguably Silicon Valley's most consistently contrarian public intellectual. He has shaped global discourse on monopoly vs. competition ("Competition is for losers"), identified the higher education bubble a decade before the student debt crisis exploded, predicted the 2008 financial crisis via macro bets at Clarium Capital, and openly challenged the tech industry's political orthodoxy by supporting Donald Trump in 2016 — a move that cost him friendships and business relationships but cemented his reputation as someone willing to break with consensus. His talks cross business strategy, technology philosophy, political theory, religious eschatology, and geopolitical analysis — making him one of the most cited and debated figures in modern tech, frequently mentioned alongside Elon Musk, Marc Andreessen, and Sam Altman as a shaper of Silicon Valley's intellectual agenda.

**Format of this timeline:** Each year lists specific talks, speeches, interviews, and appearances in chronological order where dates are known. Entries include the venue, topic, key quotes where available, and context for why the talk matters. The timeline is organised into seven eras reflecting Thiel's evolving focus: Pre-PayPal / Early Career (pre-2004), Post-Paypal / Early Investing (2004-2008), Zero to One Era / CS183 (2009-2012), Mainstream Fame / Book Tour (2013-2015), Political Engagement (2016-2018), Geopolitics / COVID / Palantir IPO (2019-2021), AI / Culture Wars (2022-2024), and Recent / Dialog Leak (2025-2026).

**Timeframe covered:** Earliest recorded speaking (~2004) through most recent public appearances (July 2026). Includes speeches, lectures, podcast appearances, conference keynotes, political addresses, extended interviews, and published essays that shaped public discourse.

### 2004–2008: Post-PayPal, Early Investing Era

**2004**
- **Stanford University** — Early talks on PayPal's founding story and startup advice. Thiel speaks at Stanford entrepreneurship events about the PayPal journey, the importance of the founding team (what would later be called the "PayPal Mafia"), and contrarian investing philosophy. Key story: how PayPal solved the chicken-and-egg problem of payments.
- **Facebook Investment** — Thiel invests $500,000 for 10.2% of Facebook (now Meta) — the first outside investment. Begins speaking about identifying outlier companies with monopoly potential and network effects. "I invested in Facebook because I saw the network effects were incredibly powerful — much stronger than anyone outside the company realised."
- **Public comments on bubble dynamics** — States the dot-com bubble has migrated into a growing bubble in the financial sector, calling out GE and Wal-Mart as vulnerable. Early articulation of his macro contrarian approach.
- **Palantir founded (2003-2004)** — Co-founds Palantir Technologies with Alex Karp, Stephen Cohen, and others. Palantir's early years are entirely stealth. Internal talks focus on counterterrorism data analysis.

**2005**
- **Clarium Capital** — Thiel's macro hedge fund returns 57.1% on his prediction the dollar would rally. Gives talks on macro investing strategy and contrarian bets. Begins building a reputation as a macro investor willing to make concentrated, non-consensus bets.
- **Stanford** — Discusses the PayPal Mafia (Elon Musk, Reid Hoffman, Max Levchin, Peter Thiel, and others), the founding story, and early lessons about network effects and payments infrastructure. "PayPal succeeded because we solved a very specific, very painful problem. We didn't try to do everything — we did one thing really well."
- **Founders Fund Launch** — Founds Founders Fund with Ken Howery, Luke Nosek, and others. The first major venture firm based in San Francisco (not Sand Hill Road). Announces investment thesis: backing "defiant optimists" building transformative technology.

**2006**
- **TechCrunch / Startup Events** — Speaks at early TechCrunch events. Early Palantir discussions begin to surface as the company emerges from stealth. Discusses the importance of government contracts for data analytics.
- **Dialog Society Founded** — Co-founds the invitation-only Dialog society with Auren Hoffman, an elite network bringing together government officials, tech executives, and academics at confidential retreats. Dialog would remain secret until a 2026 data leak exposed its membership of over 200 leaders including Elon Musk, Jared Kushner, and JD Vance.
- **Founders Fund portfolio building** — Founders Fund makes early investments in Facebook (lead investor, Series A), LinkedIn (co-invested with Sequoia), and Yelp. Thiel speaks about the venture capital power law.

**2007**
- **Venture Capital & Hedge Fund Talks** — Speaks at investor conferences on Founders Fund's investment philosophy: backing ambitious technology companies rather than "fast follower" copycats. Expresses the view that most VCs are too focused on incremental innovation: "Everyone is trying to build a slightly better mousetrap. We want companies that make the cat obsolete."
- **Clarium strategy talks** — Discusses Clarium's strategy of making concentrated macro bets rather than diversifying. Contrasts his approach with standard hedge fund practice. Clarium peaks at over $7 billion in assets under management.
- **"The Straussian Moment" (essay)** — Publishes the seminal essay in which he argues modernity has reversed the classical understanding of intellect and will. Ancient philosophers believed in the power of intellect and weakness of will; moderns believe the opposite — that will is powerful but truth is relative. This essay prefigures much of his later political and philosophical thinking.

**2008**
- **Financial Crisis Commentary** — Clarium Capital profits from the 2008 crash (up ~20% for the year while most hedge funds lost money). Thiel gives talks on macro investing during the crisis, why the financial system is structurally fragile, gold as a portfolio hedge, and the dangers of government bailout culture.
- **Oct 10 — Charlie Rose Economy Panel** — Thiel appears on Charlie Rose with a panel of journalists to discuss the financial crisis. One of his first major television appearances.
- **Key quote (2008):** "The bailout culture creates moral hazard. We are not addressing the root causes of the crisis — we are just kicking the can down the road."
- Begins developing the thesis that Western financial institutions are fundamentally broken, which later informs his political evolution toward supporting Trump.
- **Investment theses talk** — Publicly discusses his contrarian bets including long gold, short financials, and his scepticism of the housing market.

### 2009–2012: Zero to One Era, CS183 Course

**2009**
- **Facebook Board Talks** — As a Facebook board member (2005-2022), Thiel speaks at tech industry events about investing in Facebook, LinkedIn, and Yelp. Key theme: network effects create durable monopolies.
- Discusses how social networking platforms benefit from strong network effects and why Facebook won the social wars while MySpace and Friendster failed. Argues that Facebook's single login, News Feed, and social graph were defensible moats.
- **Founders Fund second fund ($250M)** — Raises Founders Fund II. Gives talks about the venture capital power law: a tiny number of investments drive the entire return of a fund. "In venture capital, 95% of the returns come from 5% of the investments. Most VCs don't understand this — they try to diversify, which is the opposite of what you should do."
- **Early Palantir growth** — Speaks about Palantir's expansion from counterterrorism to financial fraud detection. Discusses how Palantir's ontology-based approach differs from traditional data analysis.

**2010**
- **Tech Conference Keynotes** — Keynote addresses at various tech events. Discusses PayPal's business model lessons: solving a specific, high-pain problem (online payments) rather than trying to do everything.
- **Sep — TechCrunch Disrupt** — **Thiel Fellowship (originally "20 Under 20") announced.** Major talk challenging the assumption that college is necessary for success. Core thesis: "College is a four-year vacation interrupted by some classes" for many students. The $100,000 fellowship funds young people to skip college and start companies.
- **Key quote (TechCrunch 2010):** "The top 20 universities in the US are producing 400,000 graduates a year. Most of them are going into debt to get degrees that don't teach them anything useful. There's a massive bubble here."
- **Stanford eCorner talk** — Discusses PayPal's business model and lessons for entrepreneurs. Emphasises the importance of product distribution over product quality.

**2011**
- **Thiel Fellowship Launch** — Extensive media appearances and talks announcing the Thiel Fellowship. Core thesis: higher education is a bubble; college is a costly signalling mechanism, not a guarantee of learning or career success. Gets intense pushback from university administrators and the education establishment. Appears on CNBC, Fox Business, NPR, and Bloomberg TV.
- **"The End of the Future" (National Review essay)** — Publishes the essay arguing that technological progress has slowed dramatically since the 1960s, and that we are living in a period of stagnation masked by information technology. Contends that people have stopped believing in the future.
- **Key quote ("The End of the Future"):** "The United States is no longer a country oriented toward the future. We have become a society obsessed with the past, frightened of the future, and paralyzed by the present. This is the defining political fact of our age."
- **Stanford & Campus Debates** — Debates with university administrators and faculty over the Thiel Fellowship. Major media coverage positions him as the face of the "anti-college" movement.
- **The Stanford Review 25th anniversary talk** — Reflects on his time as a founding editor and the importance of intellectual diversity on campus.

**2012 (Spring) — CS183: Startup (Stanford University)**

The most significant public intellectual output of Thiel's career. An 18-lecture course at Stanford University's Computer Science department, recorded and transcribed by student **Blake Masters**. The notes became the most widely circulated startup curriculum in Silicon Valley history and evolved into the international bestseller *Zero to One*.

| Class | Title | Key Topic |
|-------|-------|-----------|
| 1 | **The Challenge of the Future** | Vertical (0→1) vs. Horizontal (1→n) progress; "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" |
| 2 | **Party Like It's 1999?** | Dot-com bubble lessons; why contrarian thinking matters in startup evaluation |
| 3 | **Value Systems** | How to think about value creation vs. value capture in business |
| 4 | **The Last Mover Advantage** | Monopoly as the goal: being the last mover in a market, not the first |
| 5 | **The Mechanics of Mafia** | Company culture; PayPal's unique founding team dynamics and hiring philosophy |
| 6 | **Thiel's Law** | The relationship between distribution and product quality; bad products with great distribution often beat great products with bad distribution |
| 7 | **Follow the Money** | VC math; power law distributions in venture capital returns — "most companies fail, but the winners return the fund" |
| 8 | **The Pitch** | How to sell your startup to investors, employees, and customers |
| 9 | **If You Build It, Will They Come?** | Distribution, sales, and go-to-market strategy — "sales matters just as much as product" |
| 10 | **After Web 2.0** | The future beyond social media and consumer internet — mobile, data, and AI |
| 11 | **Secrets** | "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" — the core of contrarian thinking |
| 12 | **War and Peace** | Competition vs. monopoly extended to geopolitics; "the peace of the monopoly is far preferable to the war of competition" |
| 13 | **You Are Not a Lottery Ticket** | Determinism vs. indeterminism; startups as planned ventures, not lottery tickets |
| 14 | **Seeing Green** | Clean energy and why cleantech largely failed — government subsidies, bad business models, and technological overreach |
| 15 | **Back to the Future** | Technological stagnation since the 1960s; why we don't have flying cars |
| 16 | **Decoding Ourselves** | Biotech, life extension, and the limits of technology |
| 17 | **Deep Thought** | AI, AGI, and the singularity — Thiel's most extensive discussion of AI to date |
| 18 | **Founder as Victim, Founder as God** | The founder's paradox; singular founders vs. institutional mediocrity |

- **Guest lecturers** included Marc Andreessen (on investing psychology and how to evaluate founders), Elon Musk (discussing SpaceX and Tesla — Musk's first detailed public account of reusable rockets), Reid Hoffman (on LinkedIn and network effects), and Bruce Gibney (on pitching).
- **Class 19 — "Stagnation or Singularity?"** was a concluding session with guest speakers from the Singularity Institute discussing AI risk.
- **Full course materials** hosted at blakemasters.tumblr.com — widely considered the single most important Thiel resource.
- Lectures also published as audio/video on YouTube via Stanford eCorner and independent uploads.
- The lecture notes were downloaded over 1 million times in the first year.

**Key lecture quotes:**
- **Class 1:** "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" — This question becomes the defining frame for his entire philosophy.
- **Class 4:** "The goal of every startup should be to build a monopoly. If you're competing, you're losing."
- **Class 11:** "There are two kinds of secrets: those that are secrets because nobody has bothered to look, and those that are secrets because nobody wants to believe them."
- **Class 18:** "The founder's vision is the single most important factor in a startup's success. Great founders are almost like gods — they create something from nothing."

### 2013–2015: Zero to One Book, Mainstream Fame

**2013**
- **Stanford Course Revisited** — Teaches CS183B: "Startup" for Stanford Continuing Studies. This version is professionally recorded and posted on Stanford eCorner, becoming one of the most-watched lecture series on the platform.
- **Oct 21 — TIFF Bell Lightbox, Toronto: "How to Build the Future"** — Major talk organised by MaRS Discovery District. Key themes: "Progress occurs through monopoly, not competition"; technology is the only way to escape the zero-sum game of globalisation.
- **Key quote (Toronto 2013):** "Competition is destructive. When you compete, you copy your competitors and you lose the ability to think for yourself. Monopolies are not just good for business — they're good for society because they allow the kind of long-term thinking that creates real innovation."
- **Early Bitcoin talks** — Begins discussing Bitcoin and cryptocurrency as libertarian tools and hedges against central banking collapse. Attends early Bitcoin conferences.

**2014 — The Year of Zero to One**
- **Sep 16 — Zero to One Book Launch** — *Zero to One: Notes on Startups, or How to Build the Future* published (co-authored with Blake Masters). #1 New York Times bestseller, translated into 30+ languages, over 2.5 million copies sold worldwide.
- **Sep 12 — "Competition Is for Losers" (Wall Street Journal essay)** — The most widely shared distillation of his monopoly thesis prior to the book. Generates massive debate in the business community. WSJ editors call it "the most provocative business essay of the year."
- **Sep 19 — The Charlie Rose Show** — Extended interview on Zero to One. Rose describes the book as "a manual for how to think about the future." Thiel discusses the innovation slowdown, the higher education bubble, and his contrarian investment philosophy.
- **Sep 29 — WBUR On Point (Tom Ashbrook)** — Radio interview discussing the book and the stagnation thesis. Partial transcript published.
- **Oct 2 — Live Talks Los Angeles** — In conversation with Peter Guber (Mandalay Entertainment CEO) at The Moss Theatre, Santa Monica. Covers monopoly theory, secrets, and the PayPal founding story.
- **Oct 10 — Google Talks (Authors@Google)** — Presents Zero to One to Google employees. Discusses how Google itself is a monopoly and why that's good.
- **Oct — New York Times DealBook Conference** — Interview with Andrew Ross Sorkin on technology and the economy.
- **Fall — Book Tour Europe & Asia** — Multiple talks in London, Berlin, Paris, Beijing, Tokyo, and Singapore adapting the Zero to One thesis for international audiences.
- **Stagnation Thesis Talks throughout 2014** — Thiel speaks extensively on why technological innovation has decelerated since the late 1960s. Core argument: "We wanted flying cars, instead we got 140 characters." Most of what we call "innovation" is actually just better information technology — not real progress in energy, transportation, medicine, or space.
- **"The New Atomic Age" speech** — Talk on energy innovation and the need for a nuclear renaissance as the only scalable carbon-free energy source.

**2015**
- **Apr — Washington Post Live: "Failing Toward Success"** — Talk at Washington Post's "Failing Toward Success" event. Discusses the importance of failure in entrepreneurship, the value of contrarian bets, and learning from startup failure.
- **Key quote (Washington Post 2015):** "The most important lesson I learned from PayPal was that it's better to solve a specific problem for a specific group of people than to try to solve everyone's problems at once."
- **May — Stanford Graduate School of Business** — Talk titled "Monopoly is the Condition of Every Successful Business." Expands on the zero to one framework for a GSB audience of MBA students and faculty.
- **Stanford CS183B recording: "Competition Is for Losers"** — Recorded lecture posted via Stanford eCorner, one of the most-watched talks on the platform with millions of YouTube views.
- **Talks on AI Safety** — Begins publicly discussing AI risk, expressing concerns about the competitive dynamics of AI development. "The problem with AI is not that it will become self-aware and kill us — it's that the race to develop it will lead to shortcuts, mistakes, and accidents."
- **Founders Fund AI investments** — Invests in DeepMind (early, before Google acquisition), Vicarious, and other AI startups. Discusses the investment rationale publicly.
- **Bitcoin/Crypto Investor Talks** — Expresses early support for Bitcoin as a hedge against inflation and government overreach. Founders Fund makes early crypto investments including a multi-million dollar Bitcoin purchase.
- **Fall 2015 — Hoover Institution engagement** — Begins participating more actively in Hoover Institution events, signalling his move toward political engagement.
- **Bishop's Event talks** — Begins a relationship with the Bishop's Event series, giving talks on technology and civilisation.

### 2016–2018: Political Engagement, Trump Endorsement, National Conservatism

**2016**
- **Jul 21 — Republican National Convention, Cleveland** — Historic speech endorsing Donald Trump. **First openly gay speaker** at a Republican National Convention. The speech shocks Silicon Valley where most tech leaders opposed Trump.
- **Full RNC speech text:** "I am proud to be gay. I am proud to be a Republican. But most of all, I am proud to be an American. I don't pretend to agree with every plank of our party's platform. But I do believe that our country is on a dangerous path, and Donald Trump offers a way forward."
- **Oct 31 — National Press Club, Washington DC** — NPC Newsmaker event. Thiel delivers a major 45-minute address explaining his Trump support. Extended Q&A with journalists from major outlets.
- **Key quote (NPC 2016):** "No matter how crazy this election seems, it is less crazy than the condition of our country. The bubble in higher education, the stagnation of median wages, the collapse of trust in institutions — these are the real crises that the political class has been ignoring for decades."
- **May — Conversations with Bill Kristol** — Extended discussion on the global economy, state of technology, and artificial intelligence. A rare in-depth political and philosophical interview before the RNC speech.
- **Post-election commentary** — Thiel becomes an informal Trump transition advisor. Speaks at several private dinners about the tech-politics divide. Discusses the future of immigration policy (H1-B visas) and technology regulation.

**2017**
- **National Conservatism Conference (NatCon), Washington DC** — **"Competition Is for Losers"** speech. Thiel's most quotable single speech. Expands on the monopoly thesis and critiques higher education in the context of American political decay.
- **Key quote (NatCon 2017):** "Competition is for losers. If you want to create and capture lasting value, look to build a monopoly. The same is true of nations. America needs to stop competing in the zero-sum game of globalization and start building things that no one else can."
- **May — NYT DealBook Conference** — Discusses the Trump administration's first year, Silicon Valley's backlash against Trump, and the political divide between tech and Washington. Calls the tech industry's response to Trump "hysterical."
- **Sep — University of Chicago, Institute of Politics (IOP)** — Extended interview on politics, technology, and the future. Covers the stagnation thesis, Trump's appeal, and America's institutional decay. One of his most politically substantive talks to a university audience.
- **Key quote (UChicago IOP 2017):** "The reason Trump won is not complicated. People are tired of being told that the economy is doing great when their wages haven't gone up in 30 years."
- **Stanford Politics interview (Nov 2017)** — Cover story: "How Peter Thiel and the Stanford Review Built a Silicon Valley Empire." Extensive interviews with over a dozen affiliates.

**2018**
- **Apr — Conversations with Bill Kristol (Filmed at Franklins, DC)** — Wide-ranging conversation on politics, tech, and the future of conservatism.
- **Key quote:** "Clean tech was not just an initial set of bad investments but why maybe ten times as much good money was thrown after bad in the 2005-2008 period. The government subsidy model created perverse incentives that distorted entire markets."
- **Oct — National Review Institute, "Future of Conservatism" Symposium** — Talk on William F. Buckley's legacy and the future of conservative thought in the age of Trump and Silicon Valley. Reflects on the relationship between economic freedom and cultural conservatism.
- **Nov 1 — NYT DealBook Conference, New York** — Calls Silicon Valley "the madness of crowds" and says "all the big ideas have been tried" in consumer internet. Predicts Trump will win re-election in 2020 if the economy remains strong.
- **Key quote (DealBook 2018):** "There is a madness of crowds in Silicon Valley. Everyone believes the same things, invests in the same companies, and supports the same politicians. That's how you get bubbles — and that's how you get political monoculture."
- Private fundraising events for Republican candidates — speaks more at political events than tech events.

### 2019–2021: Palantir IPO, Geopolitical Thinking, COVID Era

**2019**
- **Mar — Stanford University** — Returns to a CS183-like format. Talks on Palantir, the surveillance debate, and the balance between privacy and security in the age of big data. Addresses controversies around Palantir's ICE contract.
- **Key quote (Stanford 2019):** "The surveillance debate is framed entirely wrong. The question is not whether you want surveillance — the question is who controls it and how it's used. Do you want China's total surveillance state or a transparent, legally-accountable one?"
- **Jun — Bishop's Event: "Technology and Civilisation"** — Talk exploring the relationship between technological progress and Western civilisation. Argues that technology without a civilisational framework leads to nihilism and social breakdown.
- **Sep 5 — Hoover Institution: "The Straussian Moment" (Uncommon Knowledge)** — Appears on Uncommon Knowledge with Peter Robinson. Discusses his 2007 essay on ancient vs. modern conceptions of intellect and will.
- **Sep 2019 — The Portal, Episode #001 (with Eric Weinstein)** — The premiere episode of Eric Weinstein's podcast. "An Era of Stagnation & Universal Institutional Failure." Topics covered: why institutions are failing, the state of theoretical physics, and the need for intellectual diversity. One of Thiel's most widely-listened-to podcast appearances.
- **Key Portal quote:** "We live in an era where the smartest people in the world are being hired by Wall Street and Silicon Valley to do things that don't matter. That's the great misallocation of talent of our time."

**2020**
- **COVID-19 Commentary Talks** — Thiel gives several talks and interviews on the pandemic. Themes: lockdowns as government overreach, the need for accelerated vaccine development (advocates Operation Warp Speed), China's role in the pandemic, and the fragility of global supply chains.
- **Key quote (COVID 2020):** "The pandemic has revealed how dependent we are on China for everything from pharmaceuticals to electronics. The national security implications are staggering and nobody in Washington is taking it seriously."
- **Sep — Palantir IPO Roadshow** — Palantir goes public via direct listing on the NYSE (ticker: PLTR). Thiel participates in roadshow presentations about Palantir's mission, government contracts, and data integration capabilities. Palantir's S-1 filing reveals Thiel controls significant voting power alongside Alex Karp and Stephen Cohen.
- **Dec — Dialogues on Democracy (Biden Center event)** — Extended discussion on democracy, technology, and China's growing global influence. A rare appearance at an event associated with Joe Biden's orbit.
- **Private Dialog retreats** — Thiel's Dialog society continues to convene influential figures. The 2020 retreat focuses on COVID response, China policy, and the US election.

**2021**
- **Mar — American Affairs Interview** — Interview on the future of work, the permanent shift to remote work, and the decline of urban centres.
- **Key quote:** "The pandemic was a catalyst for changes that were already happening — remote work, the hollowing out of cities, the collapse of higher education's value proposition. But these changes are not necessarily good. Remote work destroys culture, connection, and the serendipity that drives innovation."
- **Jun — Bitcoin/Crypto Industry Talks** — Speaks at crypto industry events. Founders Fund was an early Bitcoin investor. Expresses support for Bitcoin as the "greatest monetary revolution since the creation of the US dollar," while expressing scepticism about altcoins and DeFi trends.
- **Nov — Bitcoin conference remarks** — Tells a conference that Bitcoin's record-high price is a sign of investors' fear about inflation: "Bitcoin is a hedge against the craziness of central banking. The Fed is printing money at a rate that should terrify everyone."
- **Mother Jones profile (Nov 2021)** — Extensive profile titled "How Dangerous Is Peter Thiel?" examines his political influence, his funding of the Trump movement, and his protégé JD Vance's Senate campaign.
- **Hoover Institution: Uncommon Knowledge** — Multiple appearances discussing the Biden administration, technology policy, and the state of conservatism in the post-Trump era.

### 2022–2024: AI Arms Race, Midterm Politics, Cultural Critique

**2022**
- **Jan — Stanford: "Competition Is for Losers" Revisited** — Returns to Stanford for a talk reflecting on Zero to One, eight years after publication. Discusses whether his ideas about monopoly, competition, and technological stagnation have held up. Addresses the rise of crypto, AI, and remote work.
- **Key quote (Stanford 2022):** "Zero to One was written before AI became the defining technology of our era. If I were writing it today, AI would be at the center of everything."
- **Feb — Meta Board Departure (after 17 years)** — Steps down from Meta's board after 17 years (2005-2022). Discusses the decision: "The board structure at Meta has evolved to the point where my presence is no longer necessary. I want to focus on other things — primarily politics and Palantir."
- **Apr 7 — Bitcoin 2022 Conference, Miami Beach** — Fiery keynote. Calls Warren Buffett "a sociopathic grandpa from Omaha" and "Bitcoin's enemy number 1." Rips $100 bills on stage. Names Larry Fink (BlackRock) and Jamie Dimon (JPMorgan) as additional enemies of Bitcoin.
- **Key quote (Bitcoin 2022):** "The gerontocracy of the financial establishment — Buffett, Dimon, Fink — they don't understand Bitcoin because they don't understand the future. They are fighting against something that is inevitable."
- **May — All-In Podcast (first appearance)** — Appears with Chamath Palihapitiya, David Sacks, Jason Calacanis, and David Friedberg. Wide-ranging: AI regulation, crypto, geopolitics, the 2022 midterms, and the future of education. One of Thiel's most candid long-form conversations.
- **Oct — Midterm Election Activism** — Pours nearly $30 million into pro-Trump candidates. JD Vance's Ohio Senate campaign receives $10 million from Thiel and $5 million from a Thiel-connected PAC. Thiel speaks at political fundraisers and private donor events.
- **Washington Post Profile (Feb 2022)** — Major profile examining his political influence and midterm spending: "Who is Peter Thiel, the tech billionaire trying to push the US to the right?"

**2023**
- **Mar — Bishop's Event: "Against Utopianism"** — Talk warning against utopian thinking in technology and politics. Argues that utopian visions (whether communist, techno-utopian, or religious) lead to authoritarianism. Makes the case for a "low-utopia" — realistic, incremental progress.
- **Key quote ("Against Utopianism"):** "Every utopia has been a nightmare. The people who promise paradise on earth always deliver hell. We need to stop trying to create heaven and instead focus on preventing hell."
- **Jul — National Conservatism Conference (NatCon)** — Keynote on **"The End of the Future" thesis**. Revisits his 2011 essay, arguing the West has lost belief in the future, manifesting in demographic decline, economic stagnation, and cultural pessimism.
- **Key NatCon 2023 quote:** "A society that has stopped having children is a society that has stopped believing in the future. We cannot have a future if we refuse to create one."
- **Sep — Aspen Ideas Festival** — Talk on AI risk, regulation, and the US-China competition. Warns that AI regulation could cede strategic advantage to Beijing.
- **Key quote (Aspen 2023):** "Every time we talk about AI regulation, we need to ask: who does this regulation help and who does it hurt? If the answer is it helps China and hurts the US, then it's not regulation — it's unilateral disarmament."
- **Ross Douthat written dialogue** — Engages with NYT columnist Ross Douthat in a published dialogue about stagnation, the "Decadent Society" thesis, and whether the West can reverse its decline.

**2024**
- **Jan — Stanford Dialogue** — Reflections on CS183, 12 years later. Discusses how the startup world has evolved since 2012.

### 3. Key Themes Across Talks

The following eleven themes recur across Thiel's speaking career, from his earliest Stanford talks through his most recent lectures in Rome and Aspen. Each theme has evolved over time — some deepened (AI, eschatology), some have been challenged by events (stagnation thesis, monopoly durability), but all remain central to his worldview.

### 1. Monopoly vs. Competition
> "Competition is for losers." — The defining Thiel thesis.

Monopolies drive innovation because they capture the profits needed to invest in the future. Competitive markets destroy profits and motivation. Every successful business is successful exactly to the extent that it does something others cannot. Thiel argues this applies to nations as well as companies — America should build what no one else can, not compete in zero-sum globalisation. "The peace of the monopoly is far preferable to the war of competition."

First fully articulated in CS183 (Class 4: "The Last Mover Advantage"), this is the single idea most associated with Thiel. He expanded it from business to politics in his 2017 NatCon speech, arguing that America itself should pursue a "monopoly" strategy of unique capabilities rather than competing in globalisation's race to the bottom.

**Key talks:** CS183 Class 4 (2012), NatCon 2017, Stanford 2022 revisit, Stanford 2026 revisit.

### 2. Zero to One vs. One to N
Vertical (0→1) progress = true technological innovation — doing something nobody has done before. Horizontal (1→n) progress = copying what works — globalisation. Thiel argues we overvalue the latter and undervalue the former. Most "innovation" is actually 1→n incrementalism. The distinction between these two forms of progress is the foundational framework of his entire philosophy.

**Key talks:** CS183 Class 1 (2012), Zero to One book (2014), "How to Build the Future" Toronto (2013).

### 3. Secrets
The central question: "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" Contrarian thinking is not just being different — it's being right when everyone else is wrong about something important. Most people stop believing secrets exist after childhood; entrepreneurs must rediscover them. "There are two kinds of secrets: those that nobody has looked for, and those that nobody wants to believe."

Thiel's most important intellectual contribution may be the framing — that the ability to identify and act on secrets is the fundamental skill of entrepreneurship. He contrasts this with the modern university system, which he argues systematically trains people to stop looking for secrets.

**Key talks:** CS183 Class 11 (2012), Stanford CS183B (2013-2015).

### 4. Technology vs. Globalisation
Technology = intensive, vertical progress (0→1). Globalisation = extensive, horizontal progress (1→n). They are sometimes in tension. Resource constraints mean not everyone can have a car; the solution is technological (electric, autonomous, shared), not just more globalisation. Thiel has consistently argued that the 1→n model (globalisation, copying, convergence) is treated as the default, but only 0→1 progress can solve humanity's fundamental challenges.

**Key talks:** CS183 Class 1 (2012), "The End of the Future" essay (2011), various 2014 stagnation talks.

### 5. The Higher Education Bubble
College is increasingly a costly signalling mechanism rather than genuine education. The Thiel Fellowship ($100,000 to skip college and start a company) was his most direct policy intervention. He argues higher education has a monopoly on accreditation that needs breaking. "College is a four-year vacation interrupted by some classes."

First announced at TechCrunch Disrupt (2010), this became Thiel's most controversial position before his Trump endorsement. It earned him intense pushback from university administrators, but the student debt crisis (now exceeding $1.7 trillion in the US) has validated many of his claims.

**Key talks:** TechCrunch Disrupt 2010, Charlie Rose 2014, various media interviews 2010-2015.

### 6. Technological Stagnation
Since the 1960s, real technological progress has slowed dramatically. We have information technology (computers, internet) but not transformative progress in energy, transportation, medicine, or space. "We wanted flying cars, instead we got 140 characters." The exception proves the rule of stagnation.

First published as "The End of the Future" (National Review, 2011), this thesis has been Thiel's most persistent macro-level argument. He has defended and refined it across dozens of talks, including his famous CS183 lectures and his 2019 Portal podcast with Eric Weinstein.

**Key talks:** "The End of the Future" essay (2011), CS183 Class 15 (2012), The Portal (2019), NatCon 2023.

### 7. AI and the Singularity
AI may be the next great frontier — or the final one. Thiel has oscillated between AI accelerationism (Palantir AIP, Founders Fund AI investments in DeepMind, Vicarious, and others) and deep concern about AI safety and control. His 2025-2026 "Antichrist" lectures frame AI regulation as a geopolitical struggle between the US and China. "The problem with AI is not the Terminator. It's that the race to develop it will lead to shortcuts, mistakes, and safety failures."

**Key talks:** CS183 Class 17 (2012), Aspen Ideas Festival (2023, 2026), All-In Podcast (2022, 2025), Tech for Good Summit (2025).

### 8. Bitcoin / Crypto
Founders Fund was among the earliest institutional Bitcoin investors. Thiel sees crypto as a libertarian escape valve from central banking and a hedge against inflation and government financial repression. He's sceptical of many altcoins and DeFi trends but remains a Bitcoin maximalist. At Bitcoin 2022 he called it "the greatest monetary revolution since the creation of the US dollar." His public Bitcoin advocacy has intensified since 2021.

**Key talks:** Bitcoin conference appearances (various, 2019-2022), Bitcoin 2022 Miami keynote, Fox Business / Bloomberg interviews.

### 9. Politics: Libertarian → National Conservative
Thiel's political evolution is one of the most tracked in American business:
- **1990s-2000s:** Classical libertarian. Stanford Review, Seasteading Institute support, donations to Ron Paul.
- **2016:** Trump supporter. RNC speech endorsement, National Press Club defense, Trump transition advisor.
- **2022:** Major GOP donor. $30M+ to pro-Trump candidates including J.D. Vance (Ohio Senate).
- **2023-present:** National Conservatism movement figure. Speaks at NatCon, critiques both parties.
He critiques Democrats as captured by woke ideology and Republicans as insufficiently nationalist. He has described himself as "a conservative who doesn't believe in conservatism" — meaning he supports disruption of sclerotic institutions but is sceptical of traditional conservative pieties about markets and tradition.

**Key talks:** RNC Speech (2016), National Press Club (2016), NatCon (2017, 2023), University of Chicago IOP (2017).

### 10. Defence Technology
Palantir is Thiel's most important long-term project. He argues the US must rebuild its industrial and defence base to compete with China. Software-defined warfare, AI-driven intelligence (Palantir AIP, Gotham, Foundry platforms), and the end of the post-Cold War peace dividend are recurring themes. He invests in defence startups including Anduril (co-founded by his former Palantir colleague Trae Stephens), SpaceX (an early Founders Fund investment), and Quantum-Systems (drones, 2022).

**Key talks:** Stanford 2019 (Palantir/surveillance), Founders Fund meetings (2020-2024), All-In Podcast (2022, 2025), Aspen Ideas Festival (2026).

### 11. Faith and Eschatology
A late-emerging but increasingly prominent theme. Thiel's Christian faith shapes his worldview more than most public commentators realise. His 2025-2026 lectures on the Antichrist represent his most explicit theological engagement, drawing on René Girard (mimetic theory, scapegoating), the Book of Revelation, and Biblical prophecy to critique modern technology and politics.

"The slogan of the Antichrist is 'peace and safety' — and that's exactly what modern technology promises. We are told that AI, surveillance, and global governance will bring us peace and safety. But the Bible warns us to be suspicious of exactly this promise."

Thiel's faith also informs his views on life extension (he funds anti-aging research), his critique of modern higher education (which he sees as a secular religion), and his scepticism of techno-utopianism.

**Key talks:** "The Antichrist" Lecture Series SF (Sep 2025), Rome (Mar 2026), Interesting Times podcast with Douthat (Jun 2025).
