## 1. Executive Summary

Singapore's economy is a highly developed, open, trade-dependent mixed market with a nominal GDP of approximately **S$680 billion (US$525 billion)** in 2025. The economy is dominated by **services (~73% of GDP)**, followed by **industry (~21% of GDP)**, with **negligible agriculture (<0.03%)**. Within industry, **manufacturing contributes ~21–22% of GDP**, one of the highest shares among advanced economies.

The key sectors—**Manufacturing, Wholesale & Retail Trade, Finance & Insurance, Business Services, and Transport & Storage**—together account for over **65% of nominal GDP** and are the focus of government industrial policy through Industry Transformation Maps (ITMs), RIE2025, and the Manufacturing 2030 vision.

### 3.4 Services Sector Ascendancy (2005–2024)

The combined services sectors (Wholesale/Retail, Finance, Business Services, Transport/Storage, Info-Comm, Accommodation/Food, Other Services) have grown from **~60% of GDP (2005) to ~68% (2024)** , with notable acceleration in:

- **Finance & Insurance**: +1.4 pp (12.1% → 13.5%)
- **Information & Communications**: +2.0 pp (3.2% → 5.2%) — the fastest gains
- **Business Services**: +1.7 pp (6.5% → 8.2%)
- **Accommodation & Food**: stable structurally but severely disrupted by COVID

### 3.5 Cyclical Shocks and Sector Volatility

- **Global Financial Crisis (2008–09)**: Manufacturing dropped 5.3 pp (25.2% → 20.5%). Finance fell from 12.5% to 11.8%. The economy contracted -0.6% in 2009.
- **COVID-19 (2020)**: GDP contracted -4.1%. Accommodation & Food halved (3.0% → 1.5%). Air transport collapsed. Manufacturing proved resilient, supported by biomedical exports. The TSL sector (Transport & Storage) fell due to air travel but maritime held up.
- **2023 Semiconductor Downturn**: Electronics manufacturing output fell -6.3% in 2023, reducing manufacturing's share modestly from 21.8% to 21.0%.
- **Inflation Surge (2022–23)**: High inflation temporarily boosted nominal shares of sectors with pricing power (Wholesale Trade, Finance) while compressing real output in others.

*Sources: SingStat Time Series on GDP by Industry (2005–2024), MTI Historical Data.*

### 3.2 Key Historical Observations

- **Manufacturing's relative decline**: From a peak of ~27% in 2005 to ~21% in 2024, though in absolute terms manufacturing output has grown significantly. The drop reflects faster growth in services sectors rather than a decline in manufacturing.
- **Finance & Insurance's rise**: Increased from ~12% in 2005 to ~13.5–14% in 2024, driven by growth in asset management and banking.
- **Information & Communications' emergence**: Doubled from ~3.2% to ~5.2%, reflecting the digital economy push.
- **COVID-19 impact (2020)**: Severe disruption to Accommodation & Food (-40% drop in share), Air Transport, and Construction. Manufacturing proved resilient, driven by biomedical and semiconductor demand.
- **Post-COVID recovery (2021–2024)**: Services rebounded strongly. Accommodation & Food recovered from 1.5% to 2.1%. Finance continued growth.

## 4. Sub-Sector Deep Dives — Top 5 Sectors

### 4.1 Manufacturing (~21.5% of GDP)

Singapore maintains one of the highest manufacturing shares among advanced economies. The sector is dominated by high-value clusters:

#### 4.1.1 Electronics (~7.5% of GDP)

- **Key segments**: Semiconductors, data storage, consumer electronics, printed circuit boards
- **Semiconductor share**: ~40% of electronics output (~3% of GDP)
- **Global position**: Singapore produces ~10% of global semiconductor output (excluding memory chips). Home to GlobalFoundries (fab), Micron (NAND), UMC, and TI facilities.
- **Growth 2023–24**: +7.8% output growth in 2024; semiconductor wafer fab investments continue (e.g., GlobalFoundries' S$4B expansion at Tampines).
- **Trend**: Cyclical but structurally growing with AI chip demand.

#### 4.1.2 Chemicals (~4.5% of GDP)

- **Key segments**: Petrochemicals (Jurong Island), specialty chemicals, petroleum refining
- **Global position**: Singapore is one of the top three global export refining centres. Jurong Island hosts >$50B in invested capital from Shell, ExxonMobil, Sumitomo Chemical, Mitsui Chemicals.
- **Output**: ~68 million tonnes of oil refining capacity annually.
- **Challenges**: Green transition pressure; government promoting sustainable chemistry and carbon capture.
- **Trend**: Flat-to-declining in relative GDP share as energy transition progresses; absolute output maintained.

#### 4.1.3 Biomedical (~3.0% of GDP)

- **Key segments**: Pharmaceuticals (active ingredient manufacturing), medical technology
- **Global position**: Major global hub for biologics manufacturing. Home to Sanofi, Pfizer, MSD, Novartis, Lonza, Thermo Fisher.
- **Growth**: Biomedical manufacturing output grew 11% in 2024. The sector has expanded 4x in nominal terms since 2010.
- **Trend**: Strong growth driven by biologics contract manufacturing and medical device R&D.

#### 4.1.4 Precision Engineering (~3.5% of GDP)

- **Key segments**: Machinery and equipment, automation, semiconductor equipment, aerospace components
- **Global position**: Singapore supplies ~50% of global semiconductor equipment consumables. Strong in aerospace MRO and oil & gas equipment.
- **Growth**: +5.2% in 2024, supported by automation demand and semiconductor equipment cycle.
- **Trend**: Growing with Industry 4.0 and regional manufacturing expansion.

### 4.2 Wholesale & Retail Trade (~16.8% of GDP)

#### 4.2.1 Wholesale Trade (~14.0% of GDP)

- **Dominance**: Wholesale trade accounts for ~83–85% of the sector's GDP contribution.
- **Nature**: Largely **entrepôt trade** — Singapore is a major transshipment, trading, and re-export hub. Trade-to-GDP ratio ~320% (2024), one of the highest globally.
- **Key Commodities**: Petroleum & petroleum products (~30% of wholesale trade), electronic components, chemicals, pharmaceuticals, machinery.
- **Global Position**: Singapore is the world's largest bunkering port (~50 million tonnes annually), and a top commodity trading hub for oil, gas, metals, and agricultural commodities (over 200 trading firms headquartered here including Trafigura, Vitol's regional HQ, Glencore, Mercuria).

#### 4.2.2 Retail Trade (~2.8% of GDP)

- **Nature**: Domestic consumption-driven, significantly supplemented by tourism.
- **Trend**: Online retail now accounts for ~18% of total retail sales (up from 5% in 2015). Brick-and-mortar retail under pressure but anchored by Orchard Road, Marina Bay, and suburban malls.
- **Growth**: Retail sales recovered to pre-COVID levels by mid-2023; 2024 growth at +2.5%.

#### 4.2.3 Entrepôt Trade Significance

Singapore's entrepôt model means that **total trade (exports + imports) is ~3.2x GDP**. Re-exports (goods that enter and leave without substantial transformation) account for ~45% of total exports. This magnifies the Wholesale Trade sector's GDP share relative to its direct value-add.

### 4.3 Finance & Insurance (~13.5% of GDP)

#### 4.3.1 Banking (~7.0% of GDP)

- Singapore is the **third-largest global forex trading centre** (after London, New York), with average daily forex turnover of ~US$1.1 trillion (2024 BIS survey).
- Three domestic banks (DBS, OCBC, UOB) are systemically important; DBS is Southeast Asia's largest bank by assets.
- Over 200 banks operate in Singapore, including all major global institutions.
- Wealth management: Singapore is the world's **largest offshore wealth hub for Asia**, with ~US$4 trillion in assets under management (AUM) as of 2024.

#### 4.3.2 Asset Management (~4.0% of GDP)

- **AUM**: ~S$5.4 trillion (2024), up from S$1.5 trillion in 2010.
- Growth driven by family offices (over 1,500 family offices established as of 2024), hedge funds, and private equity.
- Singapore is now the **third-largest wealth management centre globally** (after Switzerland and the US).

#### 4.3.3 Insurance (~1.5% of GDP)

- Life and general insurance premiums total ~S$40 billion annually.
- Singapore is a major reinsurance hub for Asia; the Insurance (Internationally Oriented) framework attracts global insurers.

#### 4.3.4 Fintech (~0.5% of GDP, rapidly growing)

- Over 1,500 fintech firms operating as of 2025.
- Key areas: digital payments (PayNow, GrabPay), digital banking (GXS Bank, Trust Bank, MariBank), blockchain, regtech.
- Singapore is the **top fintech hub in APAC** (excluding China), per the Global Fintech Index.

### 4.4 Business Services (~8.2% of GDP)

#### 4.4.1 IT & Software Services (~3.0% of GDP)

- Growing rapidly at ~8–10% annually.
- Includes software development, cloud services, cybersecurity, AI/data analytics.
- Major tech companies (Google, Meta, Amazon, Microsoft, ByteDance) have significant APAC HQs in Singapore.

#### 4.4.2 Legal, Accounting, Consulting (~3.5% of GDP)

- Singapore is a top-5 international arbitration hub globally (with SIAC hearing >600 cases/year).
- The Big Four (PwC, Deloitte, EY, KPMG) and top law firms have major regional operations.
- Consulting services tied to Singapore's role as a regional HQ hub (~4,200 MNC regional HQs as of 2024).

#### 4.4.3 Corporate HQ & Management Services (~1.7% of GDP)

- Over 4,200 multinational corporations maintain regional headquarters in Singapore.
- The International Headquarters (IHQ) and Regional HQ (RHQ) tax incentive schemes drive this cluster.

### 4.5 Transport & Storage (~6.9% of GDP)

#### 4.5.1 Maritime & Port (~3.5% of GDP)

- **Port of Singapore**: World's **busiest transshipment port** by container throughput.
- **Throughput**: 39.1 million TEUs (2024), second busiest globally after Shanghai by total volume.
- **PSA International**: Operates Singapore's container terminals; also operates ports in 26 countries.
- **Maritime services**: Ship registry (5,000+ vessels, ~100M GT), shipbroking, marine insurance, bunkering.
- **Growth**: +8.5% container traffic growth in 2024; expansion of Tuas Mega Port (remaining phases to 2040s) will increase capacity to 65M TEUs.
- **Key Metrics**:
  - Total vessel arrivals: ~2.9 billion GT (2024)
  - Ship registry: Over 5,000 vessels, ~100 million GT — one of the world's largest
  - Bunker fuel sales: ~50 million tonnes annually (world's largest bunkering port)
  - Maritime legal & arbitration: Singapore is a top-3 seat for maritime arbitration (SMART/SIMA)
  - Maritime cluster employment: ~170,000 workers

#### 4.5.2 Air Freight & Aviation (~2.0% of GDP)

- **Changi Airport**: Handled 1.9 million tonnes of air freight in 2024. Top-10 global air cargo hub.
- **Passenger traffic**: Recovered to ~70M passengers in 2024 (85% of pre-COVID peak of 68.3M in 2019).
- **Changi East construction**: T5 (fifth terminal) will add 50M capacity when operational (~2035), expanding total airport capacity to ~135M passengers annually.
- **Aviation MRO**: Singapore is the largest aerospace MRO hub in Asia-Pacific, with over 140 aerospace companies employing ~22,000 workers. MRO output valued at S$8B+ annually.
- **Air connectivity**: Changi connects to ~140 cities across ~50 countries via ~80 airlines.
- **Cargo-specific**: Changi Airfreight Centre handles ~1.9M tonnes; growing e-commerce air freight (Alibaba, Amazon regional gateways).
- **Singapore Airlines (SIA)**: Nation's flag carrier, consistently rated among world's best, operates one of the youngest fleets globally.

#### 4.5.3 Logistics & Warehousing (~1.4% of GDP)

- Singapore ranks **1st in the World Bank Logistics Performance Index** for Asia (2023, 4th globally).
- Over 7,000 logistics firms, including DHL, FedEx, UPS regional hubs.
- Growing e-commerce logistics and cold chain capabilities.

## 5. Singapore's Economic Pillars

### 5.1 The "Four Pillars" Framework

Singapore's economic strategy has traditionally been built around four key pillars, though the narrative has evolved:

#### Pillar 1: Manufacturing
- Target: Maintain 20–25% of GDP (Manufacturing 2030 goal)
- Focus: High-value, knowledge-intensive, R&D-driven
- Clusters: Electronics (semiconductors), Chemicals (petrochemicals), Biomedical, Precision Engineering

#### Pillar 2: Trade (Wholesale & Retail)
- Nature: Entrepôt trade, commodity trading, re-export
- Key: Port of Singapore, Changi Airport, FTAs (31 bilateral/regional)
- Strategy: Digitalise trade (TradeTrust, NTT), expand green trade

#### Pillar 3: Financial Services
- Focus: Asset management, forex, insurance, fintech, green finance
- Goal: Deepen capital markets, attract family offices, lead in sustainable finance
- Regulator: MAS — consistently rated among top central banks globally

#### Pillar 4: Digital Economy / Info-Communications *(Emerged as the Fourth Pillar since ~2020)*
- Digital economy estimated at **~12% of GDP** (2024), targeting **~15% by 2030**
- Includes: ICT services, e-commerce, digital media, data centre services
- Smart Nation initiative driving digital government, AI, and IoT

### 5.2 Key Cluster Evolution

| Era | Dominant Clusters | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| 1960s–70s | Labour-intensive manufacturing | Industrialisation, FDI attraction (EDB) |
| 1980s–90s | Capital-intensive manufacturing, financial services | Technological upgrading, regional HQ hub |
| 2000s–2010s | High-tech manufacturing, wealth management, biomedical | RISE, R&D push, biomedical sciences initiative |
| 2020s–present | Digital economy, green economy, AI, advanced manufacturing | Smart Nation, Green Plan 2030, RIE2025 |

## 6. Government Strategy & Policy Frameworks

### 6.1 Industry Transformation Maps (ITMs)

Launched in 2016, the ITM programme covers **23 industry clusters**. Each ITM is co-created with industry partners, trade associations, and government agencies (MTI, EDB, Enterprise Singapore, SkillsFuture Singapore, SPRING Singapore). The key pillars of each ITM are:

1. **Productivity**: Drive innovation, automation, and digitalisation
2. **Jobs & Skills**: Reskill/upskill workers, improve job quality
3. **Trade & Internationalisation**: Expand market access via FTAs and overseas networks

Key ITMs and their outcomes:

| ITM Cluster | Target Outcomes | Status (2024) |
|---|---|---|
| Manufacturing | Grow manufacturing value-add to ~S$150B by 2030 | On track (S$130B in 2024) |
| Financial Services | Maintain 4–5% annual value-add growth | Achieved (4.3% CAGR 2016–24) |
| Trade & Logistics | Boost total trade to S$1.6 trillion | S$1.4 trillion (2024) |
| Built Environment | Digitalise 80% of construction firms | ~60% digitalised |
| Food Services | Improve productivity 3% annually | ~2.5% achieved |
| Retail | E-commerce share to reach 15% | ~18% achieved (exceeded) |
| Hotels | Improve RevPAR growth | 3.5% annual RevPAR growth |
| Land Transport | Raise public transport mode share | 67% peak-hour ridership |
| Precision Engineering | Grow cluster value-add to S$24B | S$22B (2024) |
| Electronics | Maintain global market share in semiconductors | ~10% of global semicon output |
| Energy & Chemicals | Grow chemicals output with sustainability | Under review for green transition |
| Aerospace | Expand MRO output to S$15B | S$8B (2024) |
| Marine & Offshore | Transition to green maritime | New sustainability measures |

Each ITM is refreshed every 3–5 years based on industry feedback and changing global conditions. The latest refresh cycle (2023–24) integrated sustainability and digitalisation as cross-cutting enablers.

### 6.2 Research Innovation Enterprise (RIE) 2025

- Budget: **S$25 billion** (2021–2025 cycle)
- Four domains:
  1. **Human Health & Potential** (S$4B)
  2. **Urban Solutions & Sustainability** (S$3.5B)
  3. **Manufacturing, Trade & Connectivity** (S$4.5B) — Advanced manufacturing, AI, robotics
  4. **Smart Nation & Digital Economy** (S$3B) — AI, cybersecurity, quantum computing
- White space funding: S$10B for investigator-led research
- R&D intensity: ~1.9% of GDP (2024); target 2.5%

### 6.3 Manufacturing 2030 Vision

- **Goal**: Grow manufacturing value-add by 50% (from ~S$110B in 2020 to ~S$150B by 2030)
- **Strategies**:
  - Attract cutting-edge investments: Semicon (advanced nodes), biopharma (biologics), aerospace
  - Deepen enterprise capabilities: SMEs upgrade via Productivity Solutions Grant (PSG)
  - Develop talent: SkillsFuture for Industry 4.0, Advanced Manufacturing Training Academy
- **Progress**: Value-add reached S$130B in 2024; on track for 2030 target

### 6.4 Digital Economy & Smart Nation

- **Digital Economy**: Contributed S$106B (~12% of GDP) in 2023 (Source: IMDA/MTI Digital Economy Report 2024)
  - ICT sector alone: ~5.2% of GDP
  - Digitalising traditional sectors: Retail, F&B, transport (Grab, Shopee)
- **Smart Nation 2.0** (2024): Updated vision focusing on AI, trust, and community
- **AI Singapore**: National AI programme launched 2018; second National AI Strategy (2022) targets AI to contribute ~S$15B to GDP by 2030
- **Data Centres**: Singapore has ~70+ data centres; lifting of moratorium (2022) with sustainability criteria

### 6.6 Tax & Incentive Framework for Sector Development

Singapore uses a targeted incentive framework to shape sectoral composition:

| Incentive | Target Sector | Benefit | Est. Annual Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pioneer Certificate Incentive (PC) | High-value manufacturing, services | 5–15 year tax holiday on qualifying income | ~S$500M |
| Development & Expansion Incentive (DEI) | All sectors | Concessionary 5% or 10% tax rate | ~S$300M |
| Financial Sector Incentive (FSI) | Finance & insurance | Concessionary 5–12% tax rate | ~S$200M |
| Finance & Treasury Centre (FTC) | Corporate treasury | Concessionary 8% tax rate | ~S$100M |
| International Headquarters (IHQ) | Business services | 5–20 year concessionary tax rate | ~S$150M |
| Global Trader Programme (GTP) | Wholesale trade | 5–10% tax rate on qualifying trade | ~S$100M |
| Maritime Sector Incentive (MSI) | Shipping, maritime | Tax exemptions on shipping income | ~S$80M |
| Research & Development (R&D) Tax Deduction | Manufacturing, infocomm | 250% tax deduction on R&D spend | ~S$400M |
| SkillsFuture Enterprise Credit | All sectors | Up to S$10K credit per firm | ~S$50M |

The total tax expenditure on economic incentives is estimated at ~S$2–2.5B annually, representing ~0.4% of GDP.

### 6.7 Sovereign Wealth Funds and Sector Impact

Singapore's two sovereign wealth funds play significant roles in sector development:

**Temasek Holdings (S$380B AUM, 2024)**
- Owns controlling stakes in key sectors: DBS (banking), Singtel (telecoms), PSA (port), Singapore Airlines (aviation), ST Engineering (defence/aerospace), CapitaLand (real estate), Sembcorp (energy/marine)
- Investment strategy shaping sector growth themes: Decarbonisation (S$20B committed by 2030), digitalisation, longevity/healthcare
- Portfolio tilted toward Singapore-related assets (~27% of portfolio in Singapore)

**GIC Private Limited (~US$800B AUM est.)**
- One of the world's largest sovereign wealth funds
- Invests globally across public equities, fixed income, real estate, private equity, infrastructure
- Directs capital toward long-term structural growth themes: technology, healthcare, green infrastructure

These state investment entities provide patient capital that supports long-term sectoral transformations, particularly in capital-intensive industries like port infrastructure (Tuas Mega Port), energy transition, and deep-tech R&D.

### 6.5 Green Economy Transition
- **Singapore Green Plan 2030**: Targets include carbon tax (S$25/tCO2 2024 → S$50-80 by 2030), solar deployment (2 GWp by 2030), electric vehicle adoption
- **Energy Transition**: ~95% of electricity from natural gas; target to import 4 GW of low-carbon electricity by 2035
- **Impact on Chemicals/Petrochemicals**: The sector faces carbon cost pressure. Government supporting decarbonisation via:
  - Carbon capture, utilisation and storage (CCUS) projects
  - Energy efficiency grants (S$300M for industrial efficiency)
  - Sustainable chemistry R&D
- **Green Economy Opportunities**: Singapore positioning as Asia's green finance hub (MAS Green Finance Action Plan), carbon trading hub (Climate Impact X)

### 8.2 Comparative Analysis

#### Singapore vs Hong Kong
- **Similarities**: Both are small, open city-states with entrepôt trading histories. Both are top-5 global financial centres. No central bank (MAS vs HKMA); currency boards/managed floats.
- **Differences**: Singapore has maintained a **large manufacturing base (21.5% vs HK's ~1%)** despite similar land constraints. HK is almost entirely services-oriented (91.8%) while Singapore successfully keeps manufacturing through industrial policy (EDB, JTC land banks, tax incentives).
- **GDP per capita**: Singapore (~US$88K nominal, 2024) > HK (~US$53K), reflecting Singapore's higher-productivity manufacturing sector.

#### Singapore vs Switzerland
- **Similarities**: Both are high-income, innovation-driven economies with large financial sectors, strong manufacturing, and neutral political positions. Both have ~13–14% finance share and ~17–19% manufacturing share.
- **Differences**: Switzerland's manufacturing is more precision-oriented (watches, machinery, pharma), while Singapore's is stronger in electronics and petrochemicals. Switzerland benefits from EU integration; Singapore leverages ASEAN + global FTAs. Both have strong wealth management sectors (Switzerland #1, Singapore #3 globally).
- **GDP composition**: Nearly identical in sectoral distribution — both are "balanced" advanced economies.

#### Singapore vs UAE
- **Similarities**: Both are small, open economies positioning as regional hubs for trade, finance, and aviation. Both have significant petrochemical sectors.
- **Differences**: UAE is still **oil-dominated** (industry 44% of GDP, largely oil & gas), while Singapore has successfully diversified away from any single resource dependency. UAE is pursuing similar diversification (Dubai as financial hub, Masdar for clean energy, logistics at Jebel Ali).
- **Key lesson**: Singapore's path from entrepôt → manufacturing → high-tech/services is a model UAE is following.

#### Singapore vs Typical Developed Economy
- Singapore's **manufacturing share (~21%) is much higher** than the OECD average (~13%) and the US (~10%).
- Its **agriculture share (~0.03%) is negligible** — lower than any developed economy.
- Its **finance share (~13.5%)** is higher than most developed economies (typically ~5–8%).
- Its **trade-to-GDP ratio (~320%)** is among the highest globally — typical for city-states but far above the OECD average (~65%).

### 9.2 Digital Economy Growth

- The digital economy is projected to grow from **S$106B (2023)** to **~S$220–250B by 2030**.
- Key drivers: AI adoption, cloud migration, digital financial services, e-commerce, data centres.
- AI alone expected to contribute **S$15–20B to GDP by 2030** (Singapore's National AI Strategy 2.0).
- Singapore aims to be a **top-10 global AI hub** by 2030.

### 9.3 Green Economy Transition Impact

#### On Petrochemicals / Chemicals
- The chemicals sector faces headwinds from global decarbonisation:
  - Carbon tax increase (S$25→S$50-80/tCO2 by 2030) will raise operating costs
  - EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) may affect exports
  - Growing pressure from investors and customers for sustainable chemistry
- **Mitigation strategies**:
  - CCUS hubs (S$1B+ investment planned)
  - Hydrocarbon-based to sustainable chemistry pivot (bio-based chemicals, recycling)
  - Jurong Island decarbonisation roadmap (2023)

#### On Energy Sector
- Natural gas dominance (~95% of electricity) expected to decline with:
  - Solar capacity target: 2 GWp by 2030 (from ~1 GWp in 2024)
  - Low-carbon electricity imports (4 GW by 2035 from Malaysia, Indonesia, Lao PDR)
  - Potential for hydrogen (ammonia) power generation from 2027
  - Nuclear feasibility study underway (2024–2026)

#### Green Economy Opportunities
- **Green Finance**: Singapore's sustainable finance market grew to S$100B+ in 2024. MAS targets Singapore as Asia's leading green finance centre.
- **Carbon Trading**: Climate Impact X (CIX) launched as global carbon exchange.
- **Green Technology**: RIE2025 allocates S$3.5B to Urban Solutions & Sustainability.

### 9.4 Trade & Investment Outlook

Singapore's trade outlook is shaped by several structural forces:

| Factor | Impact | Direction |
|---|---|---|
| **US-China decoupling** | Singapore as neutral intermediary; increased FDI as supply chains shift to ASEAN | Positive |
| **Supply chain diversification (China+1)** | Benefits Singapore's logistics, manufacturing, and financial intermediation | Positive |
| **RCEP & CPTPP** | Expanding trade access across 21+ Asia-Pacific economies | Positive |
| **Digital trade agreements** | DEA (Digital Economy Agreements) with Australia, UK, S. Korea, EU (under negotiation) | Positive |
| **Near-shoring to ASEAN** | Singapore as hub for regional manufacturing networks | Positive |
| **Trade fragmentation risk** | Potential deglobalisation could harm entrepôt model | Negative |
| **Maritime decarbonisation** | IMO 2030/2050 targets may raise costs for bunkering, shipping | Mixed |

- **Investment Commitments** (2024): EDB secured S$17.5B in investment commitments, expected to create ~25,000 jobs over 5–10 years. These are concentrated in electronics (S$5B+), R&D (S$4B), and HQ services (S$3B).
- **Global Competitiveness**: Singapore ranked **1st globally** in the IMD World Competitiveness Yearbook (2024), driven by government efficiency, business legislation, and infrastructure.

### 9.5 AI, Robotics, and Advanced Manufacturing

- **AI in Manufacturing**: AI-powered quality control, predictive maintenance, digital twins are being adopted; the Advanced Manufacturing Centre of Excellence (AMCE) driving adoption.
- **Robotics**: Singapore has the **highest robot density in manufacturing globally** (~730 robots per 10,000 employees, IFR 2024), ahead of South Korea (610).
- **Semiconductor Outlook**: Singapore is positioning to capture advanced packaging and specialty chip manufacturing as global supply chains diversify (China+1 strategy).
- **Industry 4.0**: The Smart Industry Readiness Index (SIRI) was developed in Singapore and is now a global standard.

### 9.5 Labour Market & Demographic Impact

- **Ageing population**: Singapore's population is ageing rapidly; by 2030, ~25% will be aged 65+. This will reduce labour force growth and push up wages in labour-intensive sectors (Accommodation & Food, Construction).
- **Foreign worker policy**: Tightening of foreign worker quotas in services sectors may constrain growth in Construction, F&B, and Other Services.
- **Automation as necessity**: Sectors with high employment share but low productivity (Accommodation & Food, Construction, Retail) will need to automate to maintain competitiveness.
